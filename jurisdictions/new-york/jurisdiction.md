## New York Mediation Statutes

### Domestic Relations Law 253(c) — Referral of Matrimonial Actions to Mediation

New York courts may refer matrimonial actions to mediation. Key provisions:

- Courts have discretion to refer contested matrimonial matters — including custody, visitation, and equitable distribution — to mediation
- Referral is not mandatory; the court considers the circumstances of the case, including any history of domestic violence
- Parties are not required to reach agreement; the mediator reports only whether settlement was reached, not the substance of discussions
- Mediation does not stay court proceedings unless the court orders otherwise

### CPLR Article 75-A — General ADR Framework

While CPLR Article 75-A does not specifically govern family mediation, it provides part of the broader statutory framework for alternative dispute resolution in New York:

- Establishes authority for courts to utilize ADR processes
- Court-annexed ADR programs operate under rules promulgated by the Office of Court Administration (OCA)
- Individual judicial districts may adopt local ADR rules consistent with statewide standards

### Part 146 of the Rules of the Chief Administrative Judge — Community Dispute Resolution Centers Program (CDRCP)

Part 146 governs the statewide network of Community Dispute Resolution Centers, which handle a significant volume of family and community mediation:

- Establishes standards for centers receiving state funding under Judiciary Law Article 21-A
- Centers must provide mediation and other dispute resolution services free of charge or at low cost
- Mediators at CDRCP centers must complete a minimum of 24 hours of basic mediation training, plus additional hours in specialized areas (e.g., family, custody, divorce)
- Centers must maintain written policies on confidentiality, neutrality, informed consent, and domestic violence screening
- The Office of Court Administration oversees center compliance and administers state funding

## Confidentiality in New York

### CPLR 4547 — Compromise and Offers to Compromise

New York's primary statutory protection for mediation communications is narrow:

- Evidence of furnishing, offering, or promising to furnish — or accepting, offering, or promising to accept — consideration to compromise a disputed claim is inadmissible to prove liability or the amount of a disputed claim
- This provision mirrors Federal Rule of Evidence 408 but is limited in scope; it protects settlement offers and related conduct, not all mediation communications broadly
- Does not create a general mediation privilege

### Part 146.6 — Confidentiality in Community Dispute Resolution Centers

For mediations conducted through CDRCP centers, Part 146.6 provides stronger protections:

- All memoranda, work products, and case files of a center are confidential and shall not be disclosed without written consent of the parties
- Mediators and center staff may not be called to testify regarding mediation communications
- Exceptions exist for mandatory reporting obligations and threats of imminent harm

### New York Has NOT Adopted the Uniform Mediation Act

Unlike many states, New York has not adopted the Uniform Mediation Act (UMA). This has significant practical consequences:

- There is no blanket statutory mediation privilege in New York
- Confidentiality protections vary depending on the program (court-connected, community, or private)
- Private mediators must rely on confidentiality agreements signed by the parties, CPLR 4547, and common law protections
- Court-connected programs may have confidentiality rules established by OCA directives or local court rules
- Mediators practicing in New York should use robust written confidentiality agreements and clearly explain the limits of confidentiality protections to all parties

> **Practice note**: Because New York lacks a comprehensive mediation privilege statute, the 2025 Model Standards' emphasis on informed consent regarding confidentiality is especially critical in this jurisdiction. Mediators must ensure parties understand exactly what protections do and do not apply.

## Mandatory Reporting in New York

### New York Social Services Law 413-420 — Mandatory Reporters of Child Abuse and Maltreatment

New York has one of the broadest mandatory reporting statutes in the nation. Key points for mediators:

**Who must report (Social Services Law 413)**: The statute enumerates specific categories of "mandated reporters," including but not limited to:

- Physicians, surgeons, dentists, dental hygienists, osteopaths, optometrists, chiropractors, podiatrists, residents, interns, registered nurses
- School officials, teachers, guidance counselors, school social workers, school psychologists
- Social workers, licensed clinical social workers, mental health professionals
- Psychologists, licensed marriage and family therapists, licensed mental health counselors
- Peace officers, law enforcement officials, district attorneys
- Day care center workers, foster care workers, child care providers
- Substance abuse counselors, alcoholism counselors
- "Any other person required to report" as designated by law

**Important for mediators**: "Mediator" is not independently listed as a mandated reporter category. However, licensed professionals serving as mediators — including attorneys, social workers, psychologists, licensed mental health counselors, and licensed marriage and family therapists — remain mandatory reporters under their professional license when acting in their professional capacity. Whether the reporting obligation attaches during mediation depends on the specific professional license and the circumstances.

**What to report (Social Services Law 413-414)**: Known or suspected abuse or maltreatment of a child under 18. "Abuse" includes physical abuse, sexual abuse, and severe neglect. "Maltreatment" includes neglect, failure to provide adequate food, shelter, clothing, education, or medical care, and excessive corporal punishment.

**Where to report**: New York State Central Register of Child Abuse and Maltreatment (SCR): **1-800-342-3720** (available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week)

**Penalties for failure to report (Social Services Law 420)**: Willful failure to report is a Class A misdemeanor. Mandated reporters who fail to report may also be held civilly liable for damages caused by their failure.

**Immunity (Social Services Law 419)**: Persons who report in good faith are immune from civil and criminal liability.

> **Practice note**: Even if a mediator is not technically a mandated reporter under New York Social Services Law, the 2025 Model Standards strongly encourage mediators to act as if they are mandatory reporters when child maltreatment is identified. Given the breadth of New York's statute and the number of licensed professionals who practice mediation, most mediators in New York likely qualify as mandated reporters through their primary professional license.

## Domestic Violence and Mediation in New York

### Family Court Act Article 8 — Family Offenses

New York's Family Court Act Article 8 governs family offense proceedings, including assault, harassment, menacing, stalking, disorderly conduct, and other offenses between family or household members:

- Family Court has concurrent jurisdiction with criminal courts over family offenses
- Courts may issue temporary and final orders of protection
- The definition of "members of the same family or household" is broad, including persons related by blood or marriage, persons formerly married, persons with a child in common, and persons in or formerly in an intimate relationship

### Mediation and Domestic Violence — Court Policies

New York courts and mediation programs apply significant caution when domestic violence is present:

- Courts generally will not order mediation in cases involving allegations of domestic violence without the consent of the alleged victim and a finding that adequate safeguards can be implemented
- OCA guidelines require domestic violence screening in court-connected mediation programs
- CDRCP centers under Part 146 must maintain written policies addressing domestic violence, including screening procedures and safety planning
- When an Order of Protection is in effect, mediation must be structured to comply with its terms — including no-contact provisions, which may require shuttle mediation or separate sessions

### Orders of Protection — Considerations for Mediators

- Mediators must determine whether any active orders of protection exist between the parties before commencing mediation
- An order of protection may prohibit direct or indirect contact, which can affect the format and feasibility of mediation
- Violation of an order of protection is a criminal offense; mediators must not facilitate any communication that would constitute a violation
- The 2025 Model Standards' provisions on domestic abuse screening and ongoing assessment are especially relevant in New York, where courts rely heavily on mediators to identify and respond to power imbalances and safety concerns

## Mediator Qualifications in New York

### No Statewide Mediator Certification for Private Mediators

New York does not have a statewide mediator licensing or certification program for private practitioners. Any person may hold themselves out as a mediator. Qualifications are established by:

- Court rules for court-connected programs
- CDRCP standards for community mediation centers
- Professional organizations and voluntary credentialing bodies

### Part 146 — Community Dispute Resolution Center Mediator Standards

Mediators serving at CDRCP centers must meet minimum training requirements:

- 24 hours of basic mediation training as a minimum
- Additional training hours in specialized subject areas (family, custody, divorce, elder mediation, etc.) as required by the center and OCA
- Apprenticeship or co-mediation experience before serving as a lead mediator
- Centers must maintain records of mediator training and qualifications

### New York City Courts — Court-Connected Mediation Standards

The New York City courts maintain their own qualification standards for mediators serving in court-connected ADR programs:

- NYC court-connected mediators typically must complete 40 or more hours of mediation training, plus additional training in family law and domestic violence
- The NYC courts may require specific experience levels, professional licenses, or subject matter expertise
- The NYC Office of Alternative Dispute Resolution Programs (within OCA) oversees these requirements

### New York State Office of Court Administration (OCA)

The OCA's Division of Professional and Court Services oversees court-connected ADR statewide:

- OCA promulgates rules and guidelines for court-connected mediation programs across all judicial districts
- Individual judicial districts may adopt additional qualification requirements for their panels
- OCA periodically reviews and updates standards for court-connected mediators

### Recommended Minimum Training (per 2025 Model Standards)

- 40+ hours of mediation training (consistent with most New York court-connected program requirements)
- Specific training in domestic abuse screening and dynamics
- Specific training in child maltreatment recognition and mandatory reporting
- Family law substantive knowledge, including New York custody and equitable distribution law
- Continuing education annually

- **New York State Dispute Resolution Association (NYSDRA)**: Statewide professional association for mediators and dispute resolution practitioners; provides training, networking, and advocacy — [www.nysdra.org](http://www.nysdra.org)
- **New York State Office of Court Administration — ADR Office**: Oversees court-connected ADR programs statewide; maintains directories of approved mediators
- **New York State Central Register of Child Abuse and Maltreatment (SCR)**: **1-800-342-3720** (24-hour hotline for mandatory reporting of suspected child abuse or maltreatment)
- **NYS Office for the Prevention of Domestic Violence**: **1-800-942-6906** (24-hour hotline for domestic violence assistance and referrals)
- **The Legal Aid Society of New York**: Provides legal assistance to low-income New Yorkers, including family law matters — [www.legalaidnyc.org](http://www.legalaidnyc.org)
- **New York Legal Services Corporation Programs**: Statewide network of civil legal services organizations serving low-income individuals in family, housing, and public benefits matters
- **New York State Bar Association — Dispute Resolution Section**: Resources for attorney-mediators, CLE programming, and practice guidance
- **New York City Bar Association — Committee on Alternative Dispute Resolution**: Resources and programming specific to NYC ADR practice
