# California Jurisdiction Module

> How the 2025 AFCC/ABA Model Standards interact with California law. This is educational information, not legal advice. Verify all citations against current statutes — laws change.

---

## California Mediation Statutes

### Family Code 3170-3173 — Mandatory Custody Mediation

California requires mediation in all contested custody and visitation cases before a court hearing. Key provisions:

- Family Code 3170: If custody or visitation is contested, the court shall set the matter for mediation before or concurrent with the hearing
- Family Code 3171: Mediation proceedings are conducted under the California Rules of Court
- Family Code 3172: The mediator may meet with the child if appropriate and in the child's best interest
- Family Code 3173: The mediator may recommend that counsel be appointed for the child

### Family Code 3160-3186 — Mediation of Custody and Visitation Issues

California provides a comprehensive framework for court-connected custody mediation:

- Family Code 3160: Each superior court shall make mediation services available for custody and visitation disputes
- Family Code 3161: The purposes of mediation include reducing acrimony, developing a parenting plan that is in the best interest of the child, and ensuring that the child's health, safety, and welfare are adequately considered
- Family Code 3162: Mediation of contested custody and visitation issues shall be governed by uniform standards of practice adopted by the Judicial Council
- Family Code 3164: Court-connected mediators must meet specific training and education requirements
- Family Code 3170: Mandatory mediation before hearing on contested custody or visitation

### Evidence Code 1115-1128 — California Mediation Act

California's general mediation confidentiality framework applies to all mediations (not just family law):

- Evidence Code 1115: Definitions — mediation, mediator, mediation consultation
- Evidence Code 1117: A mediation is initiated by agreement of the parties or court referral
- Evidence Code 1118: Conclusion of mediation — by agreement, mediator declaration, or party withdrawal
- Evidence Code 1119: No evidence of anything said or any writing prepared in connection with mediation is admissible or subject to discovery
- Evidence Code 1121: A mediator's report, opinion, recommendation, or finding is not admissible

## Confidentiality in California

### Evidence Code 1115-1128 — Strong Mediation Confidentiality

California is widely recognized as having among the strongest mediation confidentiality protections in the nation. Key provisions:

- **Evidence Code 1119**: All communications, negotiations, and settlement discussions during mediation are confidential and inadmissible in any subsequent proceeding
- **Evidence Code 1119(b)**: No writing prepared for or during mediation is admissible or subject to discovery
- **Evidence Code 1120**: Evidence otherwise admissible outside mediation does not become inadmissible merely because it was presented during mediation
- **Evidence Code 1122**: Communications may be disclosed only if all parties and the mediator expressly agree in writing, or the communication is specifically permitted or required by statute
- **Evidence Code 1125**: Confidentiality protections apply even after mediation concludes

### Cassel v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 113

This landmark California Supreme Court decision reinforced and expanded mediation confidentiality protections:

- The court held that private attorney-client discussions during mediation regarding the mediation are protected by mediation confidentiality
- Even communications between a party and their own attorney concerning mediation strategy are inadmissible
- This ruling significantly limits legal malpractice claims arising from mediation conduct

### Exceptions to Confidentiality

California's strong protections do have statutory exceptions:

- **Evidence Code 1122**: Disclosure by express written agreement of all parties and the mediator
- **Evidence Code 1123**: A signed, written settlement agreement reached during mediation is admissible to prove or enforce the agreement
- **Mandatory reporting**: Child abuse and neglect reporting obligations override mediation confidentiality (Penal Code 11164-11174.3)
- **Threats of violence**: Communications involving threats of bodily harm may be disclosed
- **Evidence Code 1126**: Mediation confidentiality does not limit disclosure required by statute (including mandatory reporting statutes)

## Mandatory Reporting in California

### Penal Code 11164-11174.3 — Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act (CANRA)

California's mandatory reporting law is one of the most detailed in the nation. Key points for mediators:

**Who must report**: CANRA defines "mandated reporters" in Penal Code 11165.7 and includes a broad list of professionals: teachers, school personnel, social workers, licensed therapists (MFTs, LCSWs, LPCCs), psychologists, physicians, nurses, counselors, clergy members, and many others.

**Important for mediators**: Whether a mediator is a mandatory reporter depends on their professional license:

- Mediators who are licensed therapists (MFTs, LCSWs, LPCCs) are mandatory reporters
- Mediators who are licensed clinical psychologists are mandatory reporters
- Mediators who are attorneys are generally not mandatory reporters under CANRA, though ethical obligations may apply
- Court-connected family mediators who hold professional licenses are mandatory reporters under those licenses

**What to report**: Known or reasonably suspected child abuse or neglect, including physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, and neglect.

**Where to report**: Reports are made to the county child protective services agency or law enforcement. Each California county operates its own child abuse reporting hotline. There is no single statewide reporting number.

- **Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline**: 1-800-422-4453 (available 24/7 for guidance and referral to local agencies)
- **County-specific hotlines**: Each of California's 58 counties maintains its own reporting line through the county Department of Children and Family Services or equivalent agency

**Timing**: Reports must be made by phone immediately or as soon as practicable, followed by a written report within 36 hours.

**Immunity**: Penal Code 11172(a) provides that mandatory reporters who report in good faith are immune from civil and criminal liability.

> **Practice note**: Even if a mediator is not technically a mandatory reporter under California CANRA, the 2025 Model Standards strongly encourage mediators to act as if they are mandatory reporters when child maltreatment is identified.

## Domestic Violence and Mediation in California

### Family Code 3181 — Separate Sessions

When there is a domestic violence allegation or protective order:

- The mediator shall meet with the parties separately and at separate times upon the request of either party or the mediator
- Separate sessions are a safeguard designed to reduce intimidation and power imbalance
- The mediator may choose to conduct the entire mediation in separate sessions

### Family Code 3170 — Court Authority to Address Domestic Violence

The court retains discretion in cases involving domestic violence:

- The court may waive the mediation requirement when domestic violence is present
- Even when mediation proceeds, the court must ensure adequate safety measures are in place
- Family Code 3044 creates a rebuttable presumption against custody to a parent who has perpetrated domestic violence within the past five years

### Protective Order Interactions

Mediators must be aware of existing protective orders:

- Family Code 6300-6389 governs Domestic Violence Prevention Act (DVPA) restraining orders
- A mediation session does not suspend or modify the terms of an active protective order
- Mediators should verify whether protective orders are in effect and ensure that mediation arrangements comply with their terms
- Staggered arrival and departure times, separate waiting areas, and support persons may be required

## Mediator Qualifications in California

### California Rules of Court, Rule 5.210 — Court-Connected Family Mediators

California has detailed statewide requirements for court-connected family mediators. Rule 5.210 sets minimum qualifications:

**Education and experience**:
- A master's degree in psychology, social work, marriage and family therapy, or another behavioral science substantially related to marriage and family interpersonal relationships
- At least two years of experience in counseling or psychotherapy with families and children

**Mediation training**:
- Completion of 40 or more hours of mediation training
- Training must include family dynamics, child development, effects of divorce on children, mediation techniques, and applicable law

### California Rules of Court, Rule 5.210(f) — Domestic Violence Training

Court-connected family mediators must complete specific domestic violence training:

- Training in domestic violence issues, including the impact of domestic violence on children
- Knowledge of local domestic violence resources and safety planning
- Ability to screen for domestic violence and implement appropriate safeguards
- Continuing education requirements include domestic violence components

### Private Mediators

California does not require licensure or certification for private mediators. However:

- Professional organizations such as the California Dispute Resolution Council (CDRC) maintain voluntary standards
- Many courts maintain mediator panels with their own qualification requirements
- The 2025 Model Standards recommend that all mediators handling child-related disputes meet training levels comparable to Rule 5.210 requirements

## California-Specific Resources

- **California Dispute Resolution Council (CDRC)**: Professional organization for mediators; maintains ethical standards and training resources
- **California Department of Social Services (CDSS)**: Oversees child welfare programs statewide
- **Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline**: 1-800-422-4453 (24/7 guidance and referral to local agencies)
- **National Domestic Violence Hotline**: 1-800-799-7233 (24/7 crisis support and referrals)
- **California Partnership to End Domestic Violence**: Statewide coalition; maintains a directory of local DV programs
- **California Courts Self-Help Center**: Resources on family law mediation for self-represented parties (selfhelp.courts.ca.gov)
- **Legal Aid Association of California**: Directory of free legal services by county
