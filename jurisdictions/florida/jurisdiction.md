## Florida Mediation Statutes

### Florida Statutes Chapter 44 — Mediation Alternatives to Judicial Action

Florida has one of the most developed mediation frameworks in the United States, with comprehensive statutory authority, court rules, and a statewide mediator certification system administered by the Supreme Court of Florida.

### F.S. 44.102 — Court-Ordered Mediation

Florida courts have broad authority to order mediation. Key provisions:

- A court may refer any contested civil action to mediation, under rules adopted by the Supreme Court
- The court may refer a case to mediation at any point during litigation, including before or during trial
- The court must consider the objection of a party to mediation if based on a history of domestic violence between the parties
- Mediation costs are generally shared equally unless the court orders otherwise
- If the parties reach agreement, the agreement may be submitted to the court as a basis for a court order

### F.S. 44.102(2)(c) — Domestic Violence Considerations

Before ordering mediation in any case, the court must consider whether a party has a history of domestic violence that would compromise the mediation process. If domestic violence is present, the court may still order mediation with appropriate safeguards or may decline to order mediation entirely.

### F.S. 61.183 — Family Mediation

Florida requires mediation in contested custody and visitation disputes before trial:

- In any contested action for dissolution of marriage involving minor children, the court shall refer the parties to mediation
- Mediation is mandatory before the court may hear a contested custody or visitation matter at trial
- The mediator must be certified by the Supreme Court of Florida as a family mediator
- The court may waive the mediation requirement upon a finding of domestic violence, child abuse, or other circumstances making mediation inappropriate
- Mediation fees are set by the court and may be apportioned between the parties based on financial ability

### Florida Rules of Civil Procedure 1.710-1.730

These rules govern the mediation process in civil cases:

- **Rule 1.710** — Mediation Rules: Establishes the framework for court-ordered and voluntary mediation in civil cases
- **Rule 1.720** — Mediation Procedures: Governs appearance requirements, the authority of parties and representatives to settle, time limits, and adjournments
- **Rule 1.730** — Completion of Mediation: Addresses reporting requirements, including the mediator's obligation to report the result of mediation (agreement, impasse, or other disposition) without disclosing confidential communications

### Florida Family Law Rules of Procedure 12.740-12.741

These rules specifically govern mediation in family law matters:

- **Rule 12.740** — Family Mediation: Establishes the requirement for mediation in contested family matters, referral procedures, qualifications, and the role of the family mediator
- **Rule 12.741** — Mediation Rules: Addresses mediation procedures specific to family law, including initial session requirements, agreements, and impasse procedures

## Confidentiality in Florida

### F.S. 44.405 — Mediation Confidentiality and Privilege

Florida has one of the strongest mediation confidentiality protections in the United States. Key provisions:

- **All mediation communications are confidential.** Except as provided in the statute, a mediation participant shall not disclose a mediation communication to a person other than another mediation participant or a participant's counsel
- **Mediation communications are inadmissible.** A mediation communication is not admissible in evidence in any judicial, administrative, or arbitration proceeding
- **The privilege belongs to the parties.** Each party to a mediation has a privilege to refuse to testify and to prevent any other person from testifying regarding mediation communications
- **The mediator has an independent privilege.** A mediator has a privilege to refuse to testify regarding mediation communications in any proceeding

### Exceptions to Confidentiality

Florida law recognizes limited exceptions to mediation confidentiality under F.S. 44.405(4):

- **Signed written agreements** — A mediated agreement that is signed by the parties and their counsel (if any) is not confidential and may be used as the basis for a court order
- **Child abuse, abandonment, or neglect** — There is no confidentiality privilege where the mediator has reasonable cause to suspect child abuse, abandonment, or neglect (mandatory reporting under F.S. 39.201 supersedes mediation confidentiality)
- **Threats of violence** — Communications evidencing an intent to commit a crime, including threats of bodily harm, are not protected
- **Professional misconduct** — Communications relevant to mediator misconduct proceedings before the Mediator Qualifications Board are not protected
- **Statutory or constitutional duty** — The privilege does not apply where disclosure is required by statute or the Florida Constitution

### Practice Note on Confidentiality

Mediators in Florida should provide a written explanation of confidentiality protections and their exceptions to all parties at the outset of mediation. This is consistent with both Florida law and the 2025 AFCC/ABA Model Standards' emphasis on informed consent.

## Mandatory Reporting in Florida

### F.S. 39.201 — Mandatory Reports of Child Abuse, Abandonment, or Neglect

Florida has one of the broadest mandatory reporting statutes in the nation. Unlike many states that limit the reporting obligation to specified professional categories, Florida imposes a **universal mandatory reporting obligation**.

**Who must report**: Any person who knows, or has reasonable cause to suspect, that a child is abused, abandoned, or neglected by a parent, legal custodian, caregiver, or other person responsible for the child's welfare. This means **every person in Florida is a mandatory reporter**, regardless of profession or relationship to the child.

**Critical implication for mediators**: ALL mediators in Florida are mandatory reporters — not by virtue of any professional license, but because Florida law requires every person to report. There is no ambiguity and no professional exemption. This obligation supersedes mediation confidentiality under F.S. 44.405.

**What to report**: Known or reasonably suspected abuse, abandonment, or neglect of a child.

**Where to report**: Florida Abuse Hotline — **1-800-962-2873 (1-800-96-ABUSE)**. Reports can also be submitted online at reportabuse.dcf.state.fl.us or by fax at 1-800-914-0004.

**When to report**: Immediately. Florida law requires that reports be made as soon as the reporter has reasonable cause to suspect abuse, abandonment, or neglect.

**Penalty for failure to report**: Under F.S. 39.205, a person who is required to report and knowingly and willfully fails to do so commits a misdemeanor of the first degree, punishable by up to one year in jail and a $1,000 fine. If the failure to report results in the death, great bodily harm, permanent disability, or permanent disfigurement of the child, the person commits a felony of the third degree.

**Immunity**: F.S. 39.203 provides that any person who reports in good faith is immune from civil and criminal liability.

> **Practice note**: Because Florida's mandatory reporting law is universal, mediators should address this obligation clearly during the informed consent process at the beginning of mediation. The 2025 Model Standards' emphasis on transparency about mandatory reporting obligations aligns directly with Florida law.

## Domestic Violence and Mediation in Florida

### F.S. 44.102(2)(c) — Court Screening for Domestic Violence

Florida law requires courts to consider domestic violence history before ordering mediation:

- The court must consider objections to mediation based on a history of domestic violence
- If domestic violence is found, the court may decline to order mediation or may order mediation with appropriate safeguards
- Safeguards may include separate sessions (caucus-only mediation), staggered arrival and departure times, the presence of a support person, and conducting mediation at a secure location

### F.S. Chapter 741 — Domestic Violence

Chapter 741 of the Florida Statutes provides the legal framework for domestic violence in Florida:

- **F.S. 741.28** — Defines domestic violence as any assault, aggravated assault, battery, aggravated battery, sexual assault, sexual battery, stalking, aggravated stalking, kidnapping, false imprisonment, or any criminal offense resulting in physical injury or death, by a family or household member against another family or household member
- **F.S. 741.30** — Injunctions for protection against domestic violence (restraining orders). Mediators should determine whether any active injunctions exist between the parties, as these may affect whether and how mediation can proceed
- **F.S. 741.2902** — In dissolution proceedings involving domestic violence, the court shall consider evidence of domestic violence in determining custody and visitation

### Screening and Safety Planning

Under the 2025 Model Standards and Florida practice:

- Mediators must screen for domestic violence before and throughout mediation
- Florida-certified family mediators receive specific training in domestic violence screening
- If domestic violence is identified, the mediator must assess whether mediation can proceed safely and, if so, what safeguards are necessary
- The mediator retains the independent obligation to suspend or terminate mediation if the safety of any party or child is at risk

## Mediator Qualifications in Florida

### Florida Supreme Court Certification

Florida has the **most comprehensive mediator certification system in the United States**, administered by the Supreme Court of Florida through the Dispute Resolution Center. Certification categories include:

- **County Mediator** — 20-hour training; mediates county court civil cases (claims up to $8,000)
- **Circuit Mediator** — 40-hour training; mediates circuit court civil cases
- **Family Mediator** — 40-hour family mediation training; mediates dissolution, custody, visitation, and other family law matters
- **Dependency Mediator** — 40-hour dependency mediation training; mediates child welfare and dependency cases
- **Appellate Mediator** — Certified circuit or family mediator with additional appellate experience

### Florida Rules for Certified and Court-Appointed Mediators (Rules 10.100-10.900)

These rules, adopted by the Florida Supreme Court, establish comprehensive standards for mediator conduct:

- **Rule 10.200** — Mediator Qualifications: Sets education, training, and experience requirements for each certification category
- **Rule 10.210** — Mediator Training Programs: Establishes standards for Supreme Court-approved training programs
- **Rule 10.230** — Mentorship Requirements: Family mediators must complete mentorship/observation requirements in addition to classroom training
- **Rule 10.300-10.690** — Standards of Professional Conduct: Governs self-determination, impartiality, confidentiality, conflicts of interest, competence, and other ethical obligations
- **Rule 10.700-10.900** — Discipline and complaint procedures

### Family Mediator Requirements

To become a Florida Supreme Court Certified Family Mediator:

- Complete a minimum **40-hour family mediation training** from a Supreme Court-approved program
- Complete observation and mentorship requirements under a certified family mediator
- Hold a good moral character as determined by the Mediator Qualifications Board
- Submit an application to the Florida Dispute Resolution Center

### Continuing Education

- Certified mediators must complete **16 hours of continuing mediator education (CME)** per two-year certification period
- CME must include training in ethics, domestic violence, and diversity topics
- Failure to meet CME requirements may result in decertification

### Florida Mediator Ethics Advisory Committee (MEAC)

The MEAC provides formal advisory opinions on ethical questions arising under the Florida Rules for Certified and Court-Appointed Mediators. These opinions are published and provide important guidance for mediators practicing in Florida.

## Florida-Specific Resources

- **Florida Dispute Resolution Center (FDRC)**: Operates under the Office of the State Courts Administrator, Supreme Court of Florida. Administers mediator certification, training program approval, and discipline. Website: flcourts.gov/resources-and-services/alternative-dispute-resolution
- **Florida Academy of Professional Mediators (FAPM)**: Professional association for mediators in Florida providing networking, education, and advocacy
- **Florida Bar ADR Section**: Section of The Florida Bar dedicated to alternative dispute resolution practice and education
- **Florida Abuse Hotline**: **1-800-962-2873 (1-800-96-ABUSE)** — for reporting known or suspected child abuse, abandonment, or neglect
- **Florida Coalition Against Domestic Violence**: **1-800-500-1119** — statewide domestic violence hotline providing safety planning, shelter referrals, and crisis intervention
- **National Domestic Violence Hotline**: **1-800-799-7233** — available 24/7
- **Florida Legal Aid Organizations**: Florida has a statewide network of legal aid providers including Florida Legal Services, Legal Aid Society of the Orange County Bar Association, Legal Services of Greater Miami, Bay Area Legal Services, and others. A centralized directory is available at floridalawhelp.org
- **Florida Mediator Qualifications Board (MQB)**: Handles complaints against certified mediators and administers discipline under the Florida Rules for Certified and Court-Appointed Mediators
