## Texas Mediation Statutes

### Texas Civil Practice & Remedies Code Chapter 154 — Alternative Dispute Resolution Procedures

Chapter 154 is the primary ADR statute in Texas, authorizing courts across all civil case types to refer disputes to mediation. Key provisions:

- **Section 154.002** — It is the policy of the State of Texas to encourage the peaceable resolution of disputes through voluntary settlement procedures
- **Section 154.021** — A court may, on its own motion or the motion of a party, refer a pending dispute to an ADR procedure, including mediation
- **Section 154.022** — If a court refers a dispute for ADR, the court must set a reasonable time for the parties to complete the procedure
- **Section 154.023** — The court may appoint an impartial third party to facilitate the ADR procedure; parties may also select their own mediator by agreement
- **Section 154.024** — The court may set reasonable fees for court-appointed mediators and allocate costs among the parties

### Texas Family Code 153.0071 — Alternative Dispute Resolution in SAPCR Cases

This statute specifically governs mediation in Suits Affecting the Parent-Child Relationship (SAPCR). Key provisions:

- **Section 153.0071(a)** — On written agreement of the parties, the court may refer a SAPCR suit to mediation
- **Section 153.0071(b)** — A mediated settlement agreement is binding on the parties if it is signed by each party and the party's attorney (if any), and it meets statutory requirements
- **Section 153.0071(c)** — A party is entitled to judgment on a mediated settlement agreement unless the court finds that the agreement was procured by fraud, duress, coercion, or other dishonest means, or that the agreement is not in the best interest of the child
- **Section 153.0071(e)** — If the parties reach a mediated settlement agreement, the court shall render an order in accordance with the agreement unless the court finds the agreement is not in the child's best interest
- **Section 153.0071(e-1)** — A party may file a written objection to a mediated settlement agreement no later than the 10th day after the date of signing on grounds that it is not in the child's best interest

### Texas Family Code Chapter 153 — Conservatorship, Possession, and Access

Chapter 153 governs child custody (conservatorship) in Texas. Mediators working in Texas family cases should be familiar with:

- **Section 153.002** — The best interest of the child shall always be the primary consideration of the court
- **Section 153.131** — Presumption that appointment of parents as joint managing conservators is in the child's best interest
- **Section 153.134** — Court shall consider evidence of family violence in determining whether to appoint parties as joint managing conservators

## Confidentiality in Texas

### Texas Civil Practice & Remedies Code 154.053 — Confidentiality of Mediation Communications

Texas provides strong statutory protections for mediation communications:

- **Section 154.053(a)** — Unless the parties agree otherwise, all matters, including the conduct and demeanor of the parties and their counsel during the settlement process, are confidential and may never be disclosed to anyone, including the court
- **Section 154.053(b)** — Unless the parties agree otherwise, all records, reports, and other documents received by a third-party facilitator during the ADR procedure are confidential
- **Section 154.053(c)** — The participants or the third party facilitating an ADR procedure may not be required to testify in any proceedings relating to or arising out of the matter in dispute
- **Section 154.073** — The mediator shall not communicate the parties' positions to the court and may only report whether an agreement was reached

### Exceptions to Confidentiality

Texas recognizes limited exceptions to mediation confidentiality:

- **Signed written agreements**: A mediated settlement agreement signed by the parties and their attorneys may be enforceable and disclosed to the court
- **Threats of harm**: Communications involving threats of bodily harm or threats to commit a crime may be disclosed
- **Mandatory reporting obligations**: Confidentiality does not override a mediator's duty to report suspected child abuse or neglect
- **Case law development**: Texas courts have addressed confidentiality in cases such as *Avery v. Avery*, 394 S.W.3d 240 (Tex. App.—Dallas 2012), examining the enforceability of mediated settlement agreements and the scope of confidentiality protections

## Mandatory Reporting in Texas

### Texas Family Code Chapter 261 — Investigation of Report of Child Abuse or Neglect

Texas has one of the broadest mandatory reporting statutes in the country. Key provisions for mediators:

**Who must report (Section 261.101)**: Any person who has cause to believe that a child's physical or mental health or welfare has been adversely affected by abuse or neglect has a duty to report. Texas law does not limit mandatory reporting to specific professional categories — it applies to **all persons**.

**Professionals with enhanced obligations**: Licensed professionals including attorneys, therapists, social workers, counselors, teachers, nurses, physicians, and clergy who have contact with children face enhanced reporting duties and potential criminal liability for failure to report.

**Important for mediators**: Because Texas imposes a universal reporting obligation, all mediators — regardless of professional licensure — are mandatory reporters under Texas law. This is broader than many states.

**What to report**: Known or suspected abuse or neglect of a child, as defined in Section 261.001. This includes physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, and abandonment.

**Where to report**:
- **Texas Abuse Hotline**: 1-800-252-5400 (24 hours, 7 days a week)
- **Online reporting**: txabusehotline.org
- Reports go to the Texas Department of Family and Protective Services (DFPS)

**When to report (Section 261.101(b))**: A report must be made no later than the 48th hour after the person first suspects child abuse or neglect.

**Immunity (Section 261.106)**: A person acting in good faith who reports child abuse or neglect is immune from civil and criminal liability.

**Penalty for failure to report (Section 261.109)**: Failure to report is a Class A misdemeanor. If the person intended to conceal the abuse or neglect, it is a state jail felony.

> **Practice note**: Because Texas has a universal mandatory reporting obligation, mediators do not need to determine whether they are "mandatory reporters" — they are. The 2025 Model Standards' guidance on mandatory reporting is fully consistent with Texas law on this point.

## Domestic Violence and Mediation in Texas

### Texas Family Code 153.0071(f) — Discretion to Decline Mediation Referral

Texas law gives courts discretion to decline to refer a case to mediation where family violence is present:

- The court may decline to refer a suit to mediation if there is a history of family violence by a party or the party's agent against the other party, a child who is the subject of the suit, or another party to the suit
- The court retains discretion to refer the case to mediation even where family violence exists, if appropriate safeguards can be implemented

### Texas Family Code Chapter 71 — Family Violence Definitions

Mediators should be familiar with how Texas defines family violence:

- **Section 71.004** — "Family violence" means an act by a member of a family or household against another member that is intended to result in physical harm, bodily injury, assault, or sexual assault, or that is a threat that reasonably places the member in fear of imminent physical harm, bodily injury, assault, or sexual assault
- **Section 71.0021** — "Dating violence" is included within protective order eligibility
- **Section 71.003** — "Family" includes individuals related by consanguinity or affinity, former spouses, parents of the same child, and persons who previously lived in the same household

### Protective Orders — Texas Family Code Title 4

- **Chapter 83** — Temporary Ex Parte Protective Orders
- **Chapter 85** — Permanent Protective Orders
- Mediators must verify whether active protective orders exist and ensure that mediation does not violate any order provisions, including no-contact requirements

### Screening and Safety Planning

Consistent with the 2025 Model Standards, Texas mediators should:

- Screen all parties for domestic violence before and throughout mediation
- Offer shuttle or virtual mediation options to avoid direct contact between parties
- Terminate mediation if safety cannot be maintained
- Be familiar with community resources for domestic violence survivors

## Mediator Qualifications in Texas

### Texas Mediator Credentialing Association (TMCA)

Texas has one of the most developed mediator credentialing systems in the United States. While the state does not impose a statutory licensure requirement, the TMCA sets widely adopted standards:

**Basic Mediation Training**:
- 40-hour basic mediation training required for most court-connected mediation rosters
- Training must cover mediation theory, ethics, communication skills, negotiation, and role-playing exercises
- Training providers should be approved or recognized by the TMCA or by the court

**Family Mediation**:
- Additional training in family mediation is strongly recommended for mediators handling SAPCR and divorce cases
- Topics include child development, family dynamics, domestic violence screening, and the impact of separation and divorce on children

**TMCA Credentialing Levels**:
- **Credentialed Mediator**: 40 hours of training plus supervised mediation experience
- **Advanced Credentialed Mediator**: Additional hours and demonstrated experience
- Compliance with TMCA ethical standards is required at all levels

### Court-Specific Requirements

- Individual Texas courts may impose additional or different qualification requirements for mediators on their referral lists
- Some courts require mediators to carry professional liability insurance
- Mediators should verify requirements with the specific court where they practice

### Recommended Minimum Training (per 2025 Standards)

- 40+ hours of basic mediation training
- Specific training in domestic abuse screening and dynamics
- Specific training in child maltreatment recognition and mandatory reporting
- Family law substantive knowledge, including Texas conservatorship and possession law
- Continuing education annually
