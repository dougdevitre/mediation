## 1. Party Discloses Abuse in Joint Session

**Situation**: During a joint session, Party A makes a disclosure suggesting domestic abuse — possibly unintentionally, possibly as a strategic move.

**Standards**: 2025-V (Domestic Abuse), 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-VIII (Confidentiality)

**Protocol**:
1. Remain calm and neutral. Do not react with alarm or judgment.
2. Acknowledge the disclosure without characterizing it ("Thank you for sharing that. I'd like to take a short break.")
3. Immediately move to separate sessions (caucus).
4. In the separate session with the disclosing party: explore the nature, context, and impact using the domestic abuse screening protocol (→ references/02).
5. Assess whether mediation can continue safely. If not, follow the suspension/termination protocol (→ references/13).
6. Do NOT disclose what was said in caucus to the other party without explicit permission.
7. Document your screening assessment and process modifications in confidential notes.

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not ask probing questions about abuse in the joint session
- Do not minimize or dismiss the disclosure
- Do not blame the disclosing party for "disrupting" the process
- Do not continue the joint session as if nothing happened

## 2. One Party Refuses Financial Disclosure

**Situation**: Party B refuses to provide complete financial information (income, assets, debts). Party A cannot make informed decisions without this data.

**Standards**: 2025-II (Informed Decision-Making), 2025-I (Self-Determination)

**Protocol**:
1. Explain that informed decision-making requires sufficient information.
2. Explain the legal consequences of incomplete disclosure (without giving legal advice).
3. In caucus, explore the reasons for refusal — is it fear, control, ignorance, or strategy?
4. Suggest that each party consult with their attorney about disclosure obligations.
5. If refusal continues: explain that you may need to suspend mediation because meaningful informed consent cannot be achieved without adequate information.
6. Consider whether the refusal is itself an indicator of economic abuse or coercive control (→ references/02).

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not force disclosure — mediation is voluntary
- Do not make the disclosure yourself
- Do not take sides on what constitutes "sufficient" disclosure
- Do not continue finalizing an agreement when one party lacks material financial information

## 3. Child Refuses to See a Parent

**Situation**: During discussions about the parenting plan, one parent reports that the child refuses to visit the other parent.

**Standards**: 2025-X (Voice of the Child), 2025-V (Domestic Abuse), 2025-VI (Child Maltreatment) (→ references/03, references/08)

**Protocol**:
1. Explore the context without assigning blame: "Can you help me understand what the child has expressed?"
2. Consider whether the refusal may indicate abuse, alienation, loyalty conflict, developmental anxiety, or a combination.
3. Recommend a child specialist, therapist, or family counselor assessment before making permanent schedule decisions.
4. Help parents focus on the child's experience rather than "winning" or "losing."
5. Consider whether a graduated or supported visitation plan could address the child's concerns.
6. If abuse is suspected, follow the domestic abuse and child maltreatment protocols.

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not determine whether the child is being "alienated" or "coached" — that is a clinical/legal determination
- Do not force the parents to agree on a schedule the child has clearly rejected
- Do not interview the child yourself unless you have specific child-inclusive mediation training (→ references/15 for training requirements)

## 4. Attorney Dominates the Process

**Situation**: One party's attorney speaks for their client, answers questions directed to the party, and controls the negotiation. The party sits silently.

**Standards**: 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-II (Informed Decision-Making), 2025-IV (Barriers)

**Protocol**:
1. In caucus with the party, check: "I want to make sure you feel comfortable speaking for yourself. Are you getting what you need from this process?"
2. Clarify the attorney's role: "In mediation, the decisions belong to the parties. Attorneys are here to advise, not to negotiate on your behalf."
3. Use structured turn-taking: direct questions specifically to parties, not attorneys.
4. Consider ground rules: parties speak first, then attorneys may add.
5. If the pattern continues after redirection, assess whether the attorney's dominance is undermining the party's self-determination.

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not confront the attorney aggressively
- Do not exclude the attorney — Standard II(C) supports their presence
- Do not assume the party is fine with the arrangement without checking privately

## 5. Party Appears Intoxicated at Session

**Situation**: A party arrives at a mediation session showing signs of intoxication (alcohol, drugs, or medication effects) that appear to impair their ability to participate.

**Standards**: 2025-IV (Barriers to Participation), 2025-XII (Suspension/Termination), 2005-IV (Competence)

**Protocol**:
1. Do not accuse the party of being intoxicated. Observe behavior.
2. In a brief private check-in: "I want to make sure you're feeling well enough to participate today. How are you doing?"
3. If impairment is apparent, reschedule: "I'm going to suggest we reschedule today's session to make sure we're all able to participate fully."
4. Ensure the party has safe transportation.
5. Before the next session, address substance use as a barrier to participation (→ references/10).
6. Consider whether this is a pattern that affects the party's capacity to mediate.

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not diagnose or label the party
- Do not continue the session if a party cannot meaningfully participate
- Do not use this observation against the party in any documentation

## 6. Party Brings Up Allegations of Child Maltreatment

**Situation**: One parent alleges that the other parent is abusing or neglecting the children.

**Standards**: 2025-VI (Child Maltreatment), 2025-VIII (Confidentiality)

**Protocol**:
1. Acknowledge the seriousness of the allegation without judging its validity.
2. Move to separate sessions immediately.
3. Review your mandatory reporting obligations for your jurisdiction.
4. If the allegation meets reporting thresholds, inform the parties that you have a legal obligation to report.
5. Provide referrals to child protective services, counseling, and legal advocacy.
6. Assess whether mediation should be suspended.
7. If you lack specific training in child maltreatment cases, do not continue mediating child-related issues. Refer to a qualified mediator.

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not investigate the allegation yourself
- Do not determine whether abuse occurred — that is for CPS and the courts
- Do not promise confidentiality that conflicts with mandatory reporting duties
- Do not dismiss the allegation

## 7. One Party Agrees to Everything Too Quickly

**Situation**: Party A quickly agrees to all of Party B's proposals without negotiation, discussion, or questions. This pattern may indicate fear, coercive control, exhaustion, or a desire to end the process at any cost.

**Standards**: 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-V (Domestic Abuse), 2025-IV (Barriers)

**Protocol**:
1. In caucus with Party A, explore: "I've noticed you've been agreeing to proposals quickly. I want to make sure these decisions feel right to you."
2. Ask whether they feel safe, whether they understand the implications, and whether they've had time to consider alternatives.
3. Screen (or re-screen) for domestic abuse and coercive control.
4. Slow the process: "Let's take some time to think about this before we finalize."
5. Recommend independent legal counsel review before signing.
6. If the pattern suggests coercion or duress, do not finalize the agreement. Consider suspension.

## 8. Party Threatens the Other During Session

**Situation**: A party makes a direct or veiled threat toward the other party during a session (in-person or online).

**Standards**: 2025-V (Domestic Abuse), 2025-XII (Suspension/Termination), 2025-VIII (Confidentiality — threat exception)

**Protocol**:
1. Intervene immediately: "I need to pause the conversation right now."
2. Separate the parties (move to caucus or end the joint session).
3. Assess the nature and severity of the threat.
4. If there is an immediate safety risk, call law enforcement if necessary.
5. Determine whether mediation can continue.
6. Threats of harm may be an exception to mediation confidentiality — review your jurisdiction's rules.
7. If continuing: implement safety modifications. If not: terminate with safety referrals.

## 9. Mediator Discovers a Conflict of Interest Mid-Case

**Situation**: During mediation, the mediator realizes they have a previously unknown relationship with one of the parties, their attorney, or the subject matter.

**Standards**: 2025-VII (Impartiality & Conflict of Interest), 2005-III (Conflicts of Interest)

**Protocol**:
1. Disclose the conflict to both parties immediately.
2. Assess whether impartiality can still be maintained.
3. If yes: obtain informed, written waivers from both parties before continuing.
4. If no, or if the conflict reasonably undermines the integrity of the process: withdraw, regardless of party consent.
5. Provide referrals to alternative mediators.
6. Document the disclosure and decision.

## 10. Party Wants to Record the Session

**Situation**: A party wants to audio or video record the mediation session.

**Standards**: 2025-VIII (Confidentiality), 2025-IX (Technology)

**Protocol**:
1. Review the confidentiality provisions in the Agreement to Mediate.
2. Check your jurisdiction's rules on recording mediation sessions.
3. If recording is not prohibited by law/agreement: discuss with both parties. All parties and the mediator must consent.
4. Discuss the implications: recording may chill candid discussion; recordings could potentially be subpoenaed.
5. If one party objects or local law prohibits recording: the session should not be recorded.
6. If a party is covertly recording, this is a confidentiality violation. Address it directly and consider whether mediation should continue.

## 11. Party Pressures Mediator to Recommend an Outcome

**Situation**: A party says, "Just tell us what's fair" or "What would a judge do?"

**Standards**: 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-II (Informed Decision-Making), 2025-VII (Impartiality)

**Protocol**:
1. Acknowledge the request with empathy: "I understand you want clarity. That's natural."
2. Explain that the mediator's role is to facilitate the parties' own decisions, not to make recommendations.
3. Distinguish from evaluative processes: "If you want a professional to recommend an outcome, that's a different process, such as a parenting plan evaluation."
4. The mediator may provide general process information or legal information they are qualified to share, but shall not provide legal advice.
5. Recommend consulting an attorney for guidance on likely court outcomes.

## 12. Cultural or Religious Values Conflict with Legal Norms

**Situation**: A family's cultural or religious practices around marriage, divorce, parenting roles, or decision-making differ significantly from the legal framework.

**Standards**: 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-IV (Barriers — cultural norms), Introduction (cultural humility)

**Protocol**:
1. Approach with cultural humility — seek to understand, not judge.
2. Acknowledge the importance of cultural and religious values to the family.
3. Ensure parties understand the applicable legal framework without imposing it as "correct."
4. Help parties explore how their values and the legal requirements can coexist in their agreement.
5. If needed, invite a cultural consultant, religious leader, or community elder to support the process.
6. Ensure that cultural practices do not mask coercive control or power imbalances.

## 13. Party's Mental Health Appears to Impair Participation

**Situation**: A party exhibits behavior suggesting a mental health condition that may impair their ability to participate meaningfully (severe anxiety, manic episodes, paranoid ideation, dissociation, etc.).

**Standards**: 2025-IV (Barriers to Participation), 2025-XII (Suspension/Termination)

**Protocol**:
1. Do not diagnose. Observe and assess capacity to participate.
2. In caucus: "I want to check in with you about how you're feeling today and whether you feel ready to continue."
3. Consider shorter sessions, breaks, support person presence, or therapeutic referral.
4. If the party cannot understand the process, articulate their interests, or make voluntary decisions, suspend mediation.
5. Recommend mental health support and suggest resuming when the party is able to participate.

---

## 14. Mediator Is Subpoenaed to Testify

**Situation**: The mediator receives a subpoena to testify or produce documents related to a mediation.

**Standards**: 2025-VIII(E) (Confidentiality — subpoenas), 2005-V (Confidentiality)

**Protocol**:
1. Inform both parties immediately.
2. Review your jurisdiction's mediation confidentiality and privilege statutes.
3. Shall not testify or produce documents without a court order if doing so would violate confidentiality obligations.
4. Consult with your own attorney about the appropriate legal response.
5. File a motion to quash if appropriate under local law.
6. This applies to all information, including digital and electronically stored information.

## 15. Party Wants Child to Choose Which Parent to Live With

**Situation**: One parent says, "Just ask my kid — they'll tell you they want to live with me."

**Standards**: 2025-X (Voice of the Child), 2025-I (Self-Determination)

**Protocol**:
1. Explain that children should never be placed in the position of choosing between parents.
2. Explain that the child's voice can be included through indirect or supported methods.
3. Reframe: "We can consider your child's perspective without putting them in the middle."
4. If the child's preference is relevant, suggest a child specialist interview or child-inclusive process.
5. Help parents focus on the child's needs, not the child's "choice."

## 16. One Party Has No Attorney and the Other Does

**Situation**: Party A has legal counsel present; Party B is self-represented.

**Standards**: 2025-II(C) (Informed Decision-Making — power disparity), 2025-IV (Barriers — self-representation)

**Protocol**:
1. The mediator should consider the power disparity and may take steps to address it.
2. Options: pause the session to allow Party B to obtain counsel; bring in a support person; adjust the process.
3. Strongly recommend Party B consult an attorney, especially before signing any agreement.
4. Ensure Party B understands their legal rights and options (without providing legal advice).
5. Monitor for dynamics where the attorney's legal knowledge creates an unfair advantage.
6. In extreme cases, consider suspending until both parties have counsel.

## 17. Party Discloses Plan to Relocate with Children

**Situation**: During mediation, one parent reveals they plan to move a significant distance with the children.

**Standards**: 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-X (Voice of the Child)

**Protocol**:
1. Acknowledge the significance of relocation for the family.
2. Explore the reasons and implications — employment, family support, safety, education.
3. Ensure both parties understand the legal requirements for relocation in their jurisdiction (notification, court approval, modification of parenting plan).
4. Help parties explore modified parenting plans that account for distance.
5. Recommend independent legal counsel on relocation law.
6. Consider the child's perspective: school, community, relationships.

## 18. Settlement Reached Under Apparent Duress

**Situation**: After reaching what appears to be an agreement, the mediator suspects one party agreed under emotional pressure, exhaustion, or fear rather than genuine voluntary consent.

**Standards**: 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-II (Informed Decision-Making)

**Protocol**:
1. Do not finalize the agreement.
2. Offer a cooling-off period: "I want to give you both time to reflect on this before making it final."
3. In caucus, check with each party: "Does this agreement feel right to you? Do you feel you had the information and time you needed?"
4. Recommend independent attorney review before signing.
5. If duress is apparent, the mediator shall not allow the agreement to proceed. Consider suspension.

## 19. Technology Failure During Online Session

**Situation**: The video platform crashes, audio fails, or one party loses internet connectivity during an ODR session.

**Standards**: 2025-IX (Technology)

**Protocol**:
1. Have a backup communication plan established before sessions begin (phone number, alternative platform).
2. If connection drops: wait a reasonable time (5 minutes), then attempt the backup channel.
3. If one party cannot reconnect: reschedule. Do not continue with only one party (except in planned separate sessions).
4. Assess whether the technology failure created any safety or confidentiality concerns.
5. If technology failures are chronic for one party, explore alternatives (phone, in-person, providing equipment).

## 20. Party Uses Mediation to Delay Court Proceedings

**Situation**: One party appears to be using the mediation process strategically to delay pending court dates, discovery deadlines, or other litigation milestones, with no genuine intent to reach agreement.

**Standards**: 2025-XII (Suspension/Termination), 2005-VI (Quality of the Process)

**Protocol**:
1. Observe patterns: repeated cancellations, raising new issues after old ones are resolved, stalling on providing information.
2. In caucus, address the concern directly: "I want to make sure this process is productive for both of you."
3. Set reasonable timelines and expectations for progress.
4. If bad faith is apparent and persistent, the mediator may terminate the mediation.
5. Inform both parties of their right to pursue other resolution processes.

## 21. Party Cannot Afford to Continue Mediation

**Situation**: Midway through the process, one party reports they can no longer afford mediation fees. The other party is frustrated by the potential delay.

**Standards**: 2025-XIII (Fees), 2025-IV (Barriers), 2005-VIII (Fees)

**Protocol**:
1. Discuss the situation privately with each party (caucus) to understand the financial picture.
2. Review your fee disclosure — were sliding scale options discussed at intake?
3. Explore options: reduced fee, payment plan, referral to court-connected free mediation, community mediation center, or legal aid mediation program.
4. Do not allow fee arrangements to compromise impartiality — if one party pays more, this must not influence the process.
5. If no accommodation is possible and the party truly cannot continue, provide referrals and suspend rather than terminate if progress has been made.
6. Document the situation in your notes (confidential) and update the fee disclosure if terms change.

## 22. Party Wants to Bring a New Partner to Mediation

**Situation**: One parent asks to bring their new romantic partner to a mediation session. The other parent objects strongly.

**Standards**: 2025-I (Self-Determination), 2025-VII (Impartiality)

**Protocol**:
1. Review the Agreement to Mediate — does it address additional participants?
2. Discuss with each party separately: why does one want the partner present? Why does the other object?
3. Explain that mediation is for the parties (and their attorneys/support persons), and the mediator facilitates the parties' decisions about who attends.
4. If the new partner's presence would undermine the other party's ability to participate meaningfully, the mediator may decline.
5. Consider alternatives: the new partner can wait outside and be consulted during breaks, or attend a specific portion of the session only.
6. If both parties agree to the partner's presence, proceed. If not, the mediator should use professional judgment to protect the process.

→ references/07-session-facilitation.md, references/04-impartiality-conflicts.md

## 23. Mediator Learns a Party Lied About Income

**Situation**: During financial disclosure, the mediator becomes aware (from the other party's documents or statements) that one party has significantly understated their income or hidden assets.

**Standards**: 2025-II (Informed Decision-Making), 2025-I (Self-Determination)

**Protocol**:
1. Do not accuse or confront the party. The mediator is not an investigator.
2. Facilitate full disclosure by asking broad, open-ended questions: "Is there any other income we should account for?"
3. Remind both parties of the good-faith disclosure obligation in the Agreement to Mediate.
4. If you have serious reason to believe disclosure is materially incomplete, consider whether the other party can make informed decisions — if not, informed consent is compromised.
5. Recommend that both parties have independent financial review (CPA, forensic accountant, or attorney).
6. If a party refuses to provide accurate disclosure after multiple opportunities, consider whether mediation should continue — self-determination requires informed consent.

→ references/12-agreement-drafting.md, references/07-session-facilitation.md

## 24. Child Maltreatment Allegation Arises Mid-Mediation

**Situation**: During a parenting plan discussion, one parent alleges that the other parent's new partner has physically abused one of the children. The accused parent denies it.

**Standards**: 2025-VI (Child Maltreatment), 2025-V (Domestic Abuse)

**Protocol**:
1. Take the allegation seriously. Do not evaluate whether it is "true" — that is for CPS/law enforcement.
2. If you are a mandatory reporter in your jurisdiction (many mediators are, especially if also licensed as attorneys, therapists, or social workers), you must report to the appropriate agency.
3. Inform both parties that you have mandatory reporting obligations and will fulfill them regardless of who made the allegation.
4. Suspend mediation to allow for investigation and to assess whether meaningful participation can continue.
5. Provide referrals: CPS, child advocacy center, children's attorney/guardian ad litem, counseling.
6. Document what was said (not your conclusions) in your confidential notes.
7. Do not share one party's statements with the other party beyond what is necessary.

→ references/03-child-maltreatment.md, references/13-suspension-termination.md

## 25. One Party Has a Cognitive Disability

**Situation**: During intake, you learn that one party has a cognitive disability or traumatic brain injury that may affect their ability to understand proposals, remember agreements, or participate fully.

**Standards**: 2025-IV (Barriers & Process Modification), 2025-I (Self-Determination)

**Protocol**:
1. Do not assume incapacity. Many people with cognitive disabilities can participate meaningfully with appropriate accommodations.
2. Assess what accommodations are needed: simpler language, shorter sessions, written summaries of each session, a support person present, visual aids, frequent check-ins on understanding.
3. Ensure the party has the opportunity to consult with their attorney, advocate, or support person at every stage.
4. Use teach-back techniques: ask the party to explain proposals back in their own words.
5. If the party cannot participate meaningfully even with accommodations, consider whether mediation is appropriate. Do not proceed if self-determination cannot be protected.
6. Consult with the party's support network (with their permission) about optimal participation strategies.

→ references/10-barriers-modifications.md, references/01-intake-screening.md
