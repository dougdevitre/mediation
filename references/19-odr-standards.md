# 19 — NCTDR/ICODR ODR Standards & AI Guidance

> Standards: NCTDR/ICODR Online Dispute Resolution Standards (2022); NCTDR/ICODR Guidance for Third Parties Using AI (2026); 2025 Family Standard IX (Technology); ISO 32122

## Purpose

The NCTDR/ICODR Online Dispute Resolution Standards (approved April 2022) provide the authoritative ethical framework for all technology-assisted dispute resolution. The companion AI Guidance (2026) extends these standards to artificial intelligence use by practitioners. Together, they form the third major standards framework applicable to family mediation practice — complementing the 2025 AFCC/ABA and 2005 ABA/AAA/ACR standards already integrated into this toolkit.

These standards were adopted as ISO 32122 by the International Organization for Standardization (March 10, 2025), giving them international recognition across 175 member countries.

> **Source**: NCTDR/ICODR ODR Standards are available at https://odr.info/standards/ and https://icodr.org/standards/

---

## Scope

The ODR Standards apply to:
- ODR practitioners (mediators, facilitators, arbitrators, coaches)
- Technological platforms, systems, and tools used in dispute handling
- ODR platform, software, and system developers and designers
- Any process employing electronic communications and ICT for dispute resolution, whether face-to-face or online

The standards are **interdependent** and must be applied together. They supplement (not replace) the ethical, legal, and technical standards for face-to-face dispute resolution.

## The Nine ODR Standards

### 1. Accessible

**Standard**: ODR must be easy for parties to find within a system and participate in and not limit their right to representation. ODR should be available in communication channels accessible to all the parties, minimize costs to participants, and be easily accessed by people with different types of abilities.

**AI Guidance**: AI tools used should be designed to accommodate users with disabilities. They should provide access according to a user's language needs and be available in their jurisdiction. AI systems should be capable of understanding and adapting to cultural nuances while communicating with parties.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] ODR platform is accessible to parties with varying abilities (WCAG compliance)
- [ ] Multiple communication channels are available (video, phone, chat, email)
- [ ] Costs to participants are minimized
- [ ] Platform does not limit parties' right to representation
- [ ] AI tools accommodate users with disabilities
- [ ] AI provides access according to language needs
- [ ] AI is culturally responsive in communications with parties

---

### 2. Accountable

**Standard**: ODR systems must be continuously accountable to the institutions, legal frameworks, and communities that they serve. ODR platforms must be auditable and the audit made available to users. This must include human oversight of: i) traceability of the originality of documents and of the path to outcome when AI is employed, ii) determination of the relative control given to human and artificial decision-making strategies, iii) outcomes, and iv) the process of ensuring availability of outcomes to the parties.

**AI Guidance**: There should be human oversight of AI-based interventions throughout the process, including administrative support, analysis, recommendations, and decisions. Practitioners must ensure the origin of documents and the path to outcomes generated by AI are auditable, and clarify the proportion of decision-making performed by humans vs. AI. Users should have access to audits of AI operations. Decisions to use AI should be weighed in light of the high ecological cost of use.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] Human oversight maintained over all AI-based interventions
- [ ] Document origins and path to outcomes are traceable and auditable
- [ ] Relative control between human and AI decision-making is documented
- [ ] Outcomes are available to all parties
- [ ] Audit trail of AI operations is accessible to users
- [ ] Ecological cost of AI use is considered in tool selection

---

### 3. Competent

**Standard**: ODR providers must have the relevant expertise in dispute resolution, legal, technical execution, language, and culture required to deliver competent, effective services in their target areas. ODR services must be timely and use participant time efficiently.

**AI Guidance**: Practitioners should have sufficient expertise not only in dispute resolution but also in the technical and ethical deployment of AI within such processes. Competence includes understanding the potential benefits of AI as well as its limitations, biases, cultural or language implications, and legal requirements and liabilities. Third parties should use AI only when it will enhance the competence of the process without compromising fairness. Practitioners should exercise sufficient judgment and due diligence when relying on AI-generated information, maintaining active oversight and independent verification. The human user retains responsibility for ensuring that decisions made based on AI outputs are accurate and lawful. The data sets upon which AI tools rely should be relevant to the cultural and legal jurisdictions in which they will be used.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] Mediator has training in technical and ethical AI deployment for dispute resolution
- [ ] Mediator understands the benefits, limitations, and biases of AI tools used
- [ ] Mediator can explain AI tools and their use to parties, counsel, and other participants
- [ ] AI is used only when it enhances the process without compromising fairness
- [ ] AI-generated information is independently verified before influencing decisions
- [ ] AI data sets are relevant to the cultural and legal jurisdictions of the case
- [ ] AI outputs are reviewed at all stages to ensure human oversight over process and outcome

---

### 4. Confidential

**Standard**: ODR providers must make every genuine and reasonable effort to maintain the confidentiality of party communications in line with policies articulated to the parties regarding: i) who will see what data, ii) how and to what purposes that data can be used, iii) how data will be stored, iv) if, how, and when data will be destroyed or modified, and v) how disclosures of breaches will be communicated and the steps taken to prevent reoccurrence.

**AI Guidance**: AI systems should safeguard party data and be transparent about how data is protected. There should be clear policies on who can view data, the purposes of AI data use, and how data is destroyed or modified. Practitioners should promptly disclose any AI-related breaches and explain remedial steps. There should be clarity in the process about the parties' role in terms of AI usage and confidentiality.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] AI data handling policies are disclosed to parties (who sees data, how it is used)
- [ ] Data storage and destruction policies for AI-processed information are documented
- [ ] AI platform confidentiality parameters are communicated to parties
- [ ] Party data entered into AI systems is not used to train the AI model without consent
- [ ] Breach notification protocols are established for AI-related data incidents
- [ ] Parties understand their own role regarding AI usage and confidentiality

---

### 5. Equal

**Standard**: ODR providers must treat all participants with respect and dignity. ODR must seek to enable often silenced or marginalized voices to be heard and strive to ensure that offline privileges and disadvantages are not replicated in the ODR process. ODR must provide access to process instructions, security, confidentiality, and data control to all parties. Bias must be proactively avoided in all processes, contexts, and regarding party characteristics. ODR system design must include proactive efforts to prevent any AI decision-making function from creating, replicating, or compounding bias in process or outcome. Human oversight is required in ODR system design and auditing to identify bias, make findings transparent, and eliminate bias.

**AI Guidance**: AI should be actively monitored by humans to avoid replicating or amplifying bias. Practitioners should be familiar with the scope and source of data used by the AI tool, and seek data on its biases and reliability. ODR systems should be evaluated periodically to ensure no participant gains a technological or informational advantage through AI use. Proactive choices should be taken to enhance empowerment and prevent disempowerment through AI tool selection and usage.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] AI tools are monitored for bias in process and outcome
- [ ] Mediator knows the scope and source of data used by AI tools
- [ ] Neither party gains a technological or informational advantage from AI use
- [ ] Marginalized or silenced voices are not further disadvantaged by AI
- [ ] Periodic evaluations assess AI for bias and reliability
- [ ] Empowerment considerations are addressed in AI tool selection

---

### 6. Fair and Impartial

**Standard**: ODR must treat all parties equitably and with due process, without bias or benefits for or against individuals, groups, or entities. Conflicts of interest of providers, participants, and system administrators must be disclosed in advance. The obligation to disclose continues throughout the ODR process.

**AI Guidance**: Selection of AI tools should include knowledge of data on their production of bias and generation of inaccurate information. AI tools should operate with human oversight to prevent, identify, and eliminate bias toward any party. Consider how AI could increase access to information and participation to enhance fairness and party empowerment. Potential conflicts of interest arising from AI algorithms or their developers should be avoided and disclosed as soon as discovered.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] AI tool selection includes evaluation of known biases and accuracy rates
- [ ] Human oversight prevents AI bias toward any party
- [ ] Potential conflicts of interest from AI algorithms or developers are disclosed
- [ ] AI use enhances rather than undermines access to information for all parties
- [ ] Authority bias from AI-generated outputs is actively managed
- [ ] AI outputs are critically assessed rather than accepted as normative

---

### 7. Legal

**Standard**: ODR providers must abide by, uphold, and disclose to the parties relevant laws and regulations under which the process falls.

**AI Guidance**: AI systems should comply with all relevant laws, regulations, and standards on data privacy, AI governance, and dispute resolution. Practitioners must keep abreast of evolving legal standards for AI and inform users accordingly. AI data sets and outputs should be geographically contextualized to ensure analyses, resolutions, or recommendations are appropriate for the parties' legal jurisdictions.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] AI use complies with applicable data privacy laws and regulations
- [ ] AI use complies with applicable AI governance regulations
- [ ] AI outputs are geographically contextualized to the relevant legal jurisdictions
- [ ] Practitioner stays current on evolving legal standards for AI in dispute resolution
- [ ] Intellectual property protections are ensured for AI inputs and outputs
- [ ] Legal liability for AI use in the process is understood and disclosed

---

### 8. Secure

**Standard**: ODR providers must make every genuine and reasonable effort to ensure that ODR platforms are secure and data collected and communications between those engaged in ODR are not shared with unauthorized parties. Disclosures of breaches must be communicated along with steps taken to prevent reoccurrence.

**AI Guidance**: AI tools used should provide strong security to protect data integrity and privacy. Parties should be identified using secure mechanisms. Any AI-related data breach must be disclosed along with corrective actions. AI tools should have a data protection plan which addresses protection, disclosure, and corrective actions accessible to all parties.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] AI platforms provide strong data integrity and privacy protections
- [ ] AI data protection plan is documented and accessible to parties
- [ ] Data breach protocols are established for AI systems
- [ ] Data retention and deletion schedules are defined for AI-processed information
- [ ] Baseline security requirements are established before adopting any AI tool
- [ ] Parties are identified using secure mechanisms in AI-assisted processes

---

### 9. Transparent

**Standard**: ODR providers must explicitly disclose in advance and in a meaningful and accessible manner: i) the form and enforceability of dispute resolution processes and outcomes and ii) the risks, costs, and benefits of participation. Data must be gathered, managed, and presented to ensure it is not misrepresented or out of context. The sources and methods used to gather data that influences AI decisions must be disclosed. ODR using AI must publicly affirm compliance with relevant AI legislation, regulations, or guidelines. ODR must clearly disclose the role and magnitude of technology's influence on restricting or generating options and in final decisions or outcomes.

**AI Guidance**: Practitioners should disclose how and when AI influences dispute management processes and outcomes, including the data sources used. Parties should be informed of the level and type of human oversight over AI use and the outcome. Practitioners should make transparent the reasons behind AI-influenced decisions, as lack of explainability may obscure errors or biases. The use of AI platforms should be disclosed in terms of how it impacts the third party's interaction with the parties.

**Practice Checklist**:
- [ ] AI use is disclosed to all parties before the process begins
- [ ] How and when AI influences the process is explained in accessible language
- [ ] The magnitude of AI's influence on option generation and decision-making is disclosed
- [ ] Level and type of human oversight over AI is communicated to parties
- [ ] Sources of data used by AI tools are disclosed
- [ ] Compliance with relevant AI transparency legislation and guidelines is affirmed
- [ ] Reasons behind AI-influenced decisions are made transparent

## Practitioner Self-Assessment Questions

The 2026 AI Guidance includes key questions practitioners should consider before deploying AI in dispute resolution. These questions also guide developers creating tools for practitioners.

### Accessible
- Who has access to the AI tool and through what means? Is access denied to some?
- How might disabilities, familiarity with AI, cost, and time constraints differentially impact the parties?
- What is the cost, terms of service, and update/maintenance policy?
- Will the AI tool integrate with other platforms used in the practice?

### Accountable
- What level of human oversight exists over the AI functions?
- Is there a track record of reliability and accuracy? Independent audit or review?
- Who is responsible for the structure of the AI system and for any harm it may cause?
- Are there redress or dispute resolution protocols if disputes arise from AI use?
- Are there humans in or on the loop as the AI is used?

### Competent
- How can AI enhance the competency, efficiency, and creativity of the process?
- What competence is needed to run a process using the AI tool and to guide parties?
- Does the AI platform make efficient use of time and do minimal ecological harm?
- Is support and training offered to users?
- Are the AI data sets relevant to the cultural and legal jurisdictions of the case?
- Does the AI system enable review of outputs at all stages for human oversight?
- How frequently should the AI system be reviewed and updated?

### Confidential
- What are the confidentiality and data security policies?
- What are the data security risks and benefits, and for whom?
- Does the platform ensure personal/professional information is not seen by unauthorized persons?
- Does the platform clearly identify how information generated is used, including for AI training?
- Is data presented out of context? To whom and how is this disclosed?

### Equal
- What changes to current protocols are needed to maximize benefits and protect vulnerable parties?
- What is the data set from which the AI learns?
- What evaluative approaches ensure bias prevention in data and programming?
- Does the AI platform treat all participants with respect and dignity?

### Fair and Impartial
- What ongoing testing ensures AI functions do not reflect bias?
- How is the accuracy of AI-generated information measured and reported?
- How can human third parties prevent authority bias from AI-generated outputs?
- Can a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system strengthen the data set for specific purposes?
- What decisions are needed about coaching one party in AI use when others don't need assistance?

### Legal
- What regulations and standards affect liability in offering AI-assisted services?
- How can you ensure AI outputs do not violate intellectual property rights?
- How does AI use conform to relevant human rights standards and treaties?
- Is the AI platform operating legally in all jurisdictions where it is used?

### Secure
- What baseline security is required for the AI tool?
- What are the data breach protocols? What protocols does the third party need?
- How long should information be retained before deletion? Do deleted data remain in encrypted backups?
- Is a data protection plan available for the AI tool?

### Transparent
- What is the process for looking "behind the curtain" at AI structure and performance?
- Are users informed of the parameters of human influence when AI is used?
- What protocols ensure transparency about AI impact on process, outcomes, and human oversight?
- What disclosures should parties make about their personal use of AI separate from the mediator's tools?
- How does the AI tool contextualize responses to the specific circumstances of the case?

| ODR Standard | 2025 Family (AFCC/ABA) | 2005 General (ABA/AAA/ACR) |
|---|---|---|
| Accessible | IV (Barriers), IX (Technology) | VI (Quality of Process) |

| Confidential | VIII (Confidentiality), IX (Technology) | V (Confidentiality) |

| Secure | VIII (Confidentiality), IX (Technology) | V (Confidentiality) |
