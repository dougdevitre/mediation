# 02 — Domestic Abuse Screening

> Standards: 2025 Family Standard V (Domestic Abuse); 2005 General Standard VI (Quality of the Process)

## Purpose

Domestic abuse is a significant barrier to voluntary decision-making in mediation. The 2025 standards require screening every case — separately and confidentially with each party — before seeking consent to mediate and continuously throughout the process.

---

## Key Definitions (2025 Standards)

**Domestic abuse** involves physically, sexually, economically, psychologically, and coercively controlling behaviors directed by or against current or former family or household members. These behaviors may occur alone or in combination and vary in frequency, recency, severity, manner, directionality, pattern, intention, circumstance, and consequence.

Categories of domestic abuse:
- **Physical**: Intentional use of physical force with potential to cause injury, harm, disability, or death
- **Sexual**: Unwanted sexual activity without consent through force, threats, deception, or exploitation
- **Economic**: Use of financial resources to diminish or deprive economic security, stability, or self-sufficiency
- **Psychological**: Intentional infliction of harm to emotional safety, security, or wellbeing
- **Coercive control**: Harmful conduct that subordinates the will of another through violence, intimidation, intrusiveness, isolation, or control

---

## Mandatory Requirements

The 2025 standards use **"shall"** (mandatory) for all of these:

1. **Screen each prospective party separately and confidentially** for past or present domestic abuse, including coercive control, **prior to** seeking informed consent to mediate
2. **Screen and monitor throughout** the mediation process, whether or not abuse was identified at intake
3. **When domestic abuse is identified**: examine the nature and context; help each party assess its impact on meaningful participation; inquire separately whether each party believes they will be safe and able to make autonomous decisions
4. **Do not mediate without specific training** on identifying the nature, context, and dynamics of domestic abuse, including coercive control and its impact on parenting, co-parenting, children, and the mediation process
5. **Obtain ongoing and updated training** on domestic abuse topics
6. **Facilitate parenting plans** that protect the physical safety and psychological wellbeing of parties and children

---

## Screening Protocol

### Step 1: Separate Individual Sessions

Conduct screening in private, one-on-one sessions with each party. Never screen for domestic abuse in joint sessions.

### Step 2: Screening Domains

For each party, explore the following areas. Use open-ended questions. Do not use yes/no checklists with the parties present — use them as your internal framework.

**Physical safety**
- History of physical violence between the parties
- Threats of physical harm (direct or implied)
- Use of weapons
- Physical harm to children, pets, or property
- Restraining/protection orders (current or past)

**Sexual coercion**
- History of unwanted sexual contact or coercion
- Use of sex as control or punishment

**Economic control**
- Control over finances, employment, housing
- Withholding access to money, bank accounts, credit
- Forced financial dependence
- Economic threats related to mediation outcomes

**Psychological/emotional abuse**
- Intimidation, threats, degradation
- Isolation from family, friends, community
- Monitoring or surveillance of communications/movements
- Manipulation of children against the other parent
- Gaslighting or reality distortion

**Coercive control patterns**
- Pattern of domination through multiple forms of abuse
- Rules imposed on daily behavior
- Punishment for "violations" of imposed rules
- Control over appearance, social contacts, activities
- Weaponizing legal/court processes

**Impact on participation**
- Fear of the other party
- Ability to speak freely and advocate for themselves
- Ability to say "no" to proposals
- History of giving in to avoid conflict or harm
- Ability to make decisions without fear of retaliation

### Step 3: Assess Impact on Mediation

If domestic abuse indicators are present, evaluate:

- Can this party make **autonomous decisions** in mediation?
- Will this party be **safe** during and after mediation sessions?
- Can this party participate in **good faith**?
- Does this party have access to **information, applicable law, and procedural options**?
- What **safeguards and process modifications** could address concerns?

### Step 4: Determine Process Design

| Finding | Action |
|---|---|
| No indicators of domestic abuse | Proceed with standard mediation process |
| Indicators present, party believes they can participate safely with modifications | Design safeguards: shuttle mediation, separate waiting areas, staggered arrival/departure, support persons, online sessions, attorney presence |
| Indicators present, barriers cannot be remediated | Do not mediate. Help parties explore alternative dispute resolution options. Provide referrals. |
| Active safety risk | Do not mediate. Provide safety resources and referrals immediately. |

---

## Process Modifications for Domestic Abuse Cases

When mediation proceeds with identified domestic abuse concerns, consider:

- **Shuttle / caucus mediation**: Parties never in the same room
- **Remote / online sessions**: Parties in separate locations
- **Staggered scheduling**: Different arrival and departure times
- **Support persons**: Allow advocates, attorneys, or support persons to attend
- **Separate waiting areas**: Ensure parties do not encounter each other before/after sessions
- **Safety planning**: Coordinate with domestic abuse advocates
- **Shorter sessions**: Reduce opportunity for escalation
- **Written communication only**: Between sessions, no direct party-to-party contact
- **Parenting plan design**: Limit opportunities for future manipulation or abuse; traditional co-parenting models may not be suitable in coercive control cases

---

## Ongoing Monitoring

Screening does not end at intake. Monitor for new indicators throughout mediation:

- Changes in party demeanor, engagement, or willingness to speak
- One party consistently deferring to the other
- New disclosures of abuse or controlling behavior
- Evidence of communication outside mediation that suggests coercion
- Changes in children's behavior or wellbeing reported by either party
- Threats or intimidation between sessions

If new indicators emerge, return to the screening assessment and re-evaluate whether mediation remains appropriate.

---

## Mediator Training Requirements

Per Standard V(C), a mediator **shall not** mediate family cases without **specific training** on:

- Identifying the nature, context, and dynamics of domestic abuse
- Coercive control and its impact on parenting, co-parenting, and children
- Impact of domestic abuse on the mediation process
- Ongoing and updated training on these topics

## Resources to Provide to Parties (as appropriate)

- National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233 / thehotline.org
- Local domestic abuse shelters and advocacy organizations
- Legal aid / pro bono family law attorneys
- Safety planning resources
- Court-based victim advocacy programs

> **Note**: Providing resources is an act of safety support, not a determination of abuse. The mediator does not determine whether abuse has occurred — the mediator assesses whether meaningful participation in mediation is possible.
