# 06 — Technology & ODR Compliance

> Standards: 2025 Family Standard IX (Technology); 2005 General Standard IV (Competence)

## Purpose

The 2025 standards — the first revision since 2000 — explicitly address technology and online dispute resolution (ODR), recognizing the transformative impact of digital tools on mediation practice, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic.

---

## Key Definitions

**Technology** in mediation: the broad and evolving array of digital tools, platforms, and systems that facilitate, enhance, and support all aspects of the mediation process, including secure communication methods, case/document management, videoconferencing, ODR platforms, data security measures, agreement drafting tools, analytical and decision-support technologies, educational resources, and emerging technologies such as AI, large language models, algorithmic decision-making tools, and similar technology.

**ODR (Online Dispute Resolution)**: use of technology to facilitate dispute resolution processes not conducted face to face — videoconferencing, phone, email, chat/text, and other online platforms.

---

## Mandatory Requirements

### Competence (SHALL / SHOULD)

- **Should** assess participants' abilities to use technology and obtain informed consent
- **Should** provide clear, accessible information about technology's benefits, challenges, and alternatives
- **Should** obtain and maintain training necessary to use technology competently
- **Should** have strong working knowledge of capabilities, limitations, and challenges
- **Should** be aware of how technology impacts engagement, communication dynamics, and decision-making
- **Should** treat all digital data with the same confidentiality as traditional mediation
- **Should** implement robust data security (including end-to-end encryption)
- **Should** conduct regular evaluations of technological tools (ethical, legal, practical)
- **Should** ensure technology serves the process, not defines it

## Technology Assessment Checklist

### Platform Selection
- [ ] Is the platform HIPAA-compliant or equivalent for sensitive family data?
- [ ] Does it support end-to-end encryption?
- [ ] Can parties participate from separate locations (shuttle/caucus capability)?
- [ ] Does it support screen sharing for document review?
- [ ] Is it accessible to parties with varying technical abilities?
- [ ] Does it comply with jurisdictional data residency requirements?
- [ ] What is the platform's data retention and deletion policy?

### Party Readiness
- [ ] Does each party have reliable internet access?
- [ ] Does each party have a private, safe location for sessions?
- [ ] Does each party have the technical ability to use the platform?
- [ ] Are accommodations needed (phone-in option, tech support, practice session)?
- [ ] Has each party given informed consent to use technology?

### Safety Considerations for ODR
- [ ] Can the abusive party monitor or record the other party's session?
- [ ] Is each party in a safe, private location?
- [ ] Could the other party be present in the room during a party's session?
- [ ] Are there covert recording risks?
- [ ] Has the mediator verified each party is alone (if required)?

### Data Security
- [ ] End-to-end encryption enabled
- [ ] Session recordings disabled (or consent obtained with secure storage)
- [ ] Documents shared via secure platform (not unencrypted email)
- [ ] Chat/messaging history protected
- [ ] Participant credentials not shared
- [ ] Retention/deletion schedule established

### AI and Emerging Technology
- [ ] If AI tools are used (drafting assistance, translation, analysis), has informed consent been obtained?
- [ ] Are AI-generated outputs reviewed by the mediator before sharing?
- [ ] Are the limitations of AI tools disclosed to parties?
- [ ] Is party data being processed by third-party AI services? If so, is this disclosed and consented to?
