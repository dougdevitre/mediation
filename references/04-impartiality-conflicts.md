# 04 — Impartiality & Conflicts of Interest

> Standards: 2025 Family Standard VII; 2005 General Standards II (Impartiality), III (Conflicts of Interest)

## Purpose

The mediator must conduct mediation free from favoritism, bias, prejudice, and conflicts of interest. This standard protects the integrity of the process and ensures both parties trust the mediator's neutrality.

---

## Mandatory Requirements

### Impartiality (SHALL)

- Decline or withdraw if unable to maintain impartiality
- Identify all actual or potential conflicts of interest or grounds of bias reasonably known
- Resolve doubts in favor of recognizing the presence of a conflict or bias
- Withdraw or promptly disclose conflicts before mediation starts (or immediately upon becoming aware)
- Withdraw regardless of party agreement if impartiality is compromised

### Conflict of Interest Disclosure (SHALL / SHOULD)

- If a conflict exists but the mediator believes impartiality can be maintained, proceed **only if** all parties explicitly agree and sign an **informed, written waiver**
- If the conflict might reasonably undermine the integrity of the mediation, withdraw regardless of party agreement
- Guard against bias based on personal characteristics, background, beliefs, or performance
- Avoid conflicts of interest when recommending other professionals' services

---

## Conflict-of-Interest Check Protocol

### Pre-Mediation Review

Before accepting any case, check for:

**Prior relationships with parties or counsel**
- [ ] Have I represented, counseled, or mediated for either party before?
- [ ] Have I had a social, business, or personal relationship with either party?
- [ ] Have I had a professional relationship with either party's attorney or other representatives?
- [ ] Am I related to or close friends with any participant?

**Subject matter conflicts**
- [ ] Do I have a financial interest in the outcome?
- [ ] Do I have personal views on the subject matter that could affect impartiality?
- [ ] Am I involved in any related case or matter?

**Institutional conflicts**
- [ ] Does my employer or contracting agency have a relationship with either party?
- [ ] Am I under pressure from a court, agency, or program to achieve particular outcomes?
- [ ] Do referral relationships create an appearance of partiality?

**Role conflicts**
- [ ] Have I served in another professional role for either party (therapist, attorney, evaluator)?
- [ ] Could accepting this case create a dual-role situation?

### During Mediation

Monitor for emerging conflicts:
- [ ] Am I developing a preference for one party's position?
- [ ] Am I reacting to personal characteristics rather than the issues?
- [ ] Has new information revealed a previously unknown relationship or interest?
- [ ] Am I being influenced by external pressures (time, fees, settlement rates)?

---

## Decision Matrix

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| No conflict identified | Proceed |
| Potential conflict identified; mediator believes impartiality can be maintained | Disclose to both parties. Proceed only with informed, written waiver from all parties. |
| Actual conflict that might undermine integrity | Withdraw, regardless of party consent |
| Impartiality compromised during mediation | Withdraw immediately, regardless of party consent |
| Bias toward/against a party based on personal characteristics | Withdraw |
| Recommending a professional with whom mediator has a financial relationship | Disclose the relationship. Consider alternative referrals. |

---

## Common Bias Traps

Mediators should be self-aware about these common sources of bias:

- **Anchoring to the first party's narrative**: Give equal weight to both accounts
- **Outcome bias**: Wanting a particular result (especially in child-related matters)
- **Cultural assumptions**: Imposing your cultural norms on families with different backgrounds
- **Gender bias**: Assumptions about parenting roles, financial responsibility, or credibility
- **Status bias**: Favoring the party with greater education, wealth, or professional status
- **Repeat-player bias**: Favoring the party whose attorney you work with frequently
- **Efficiency bias**: Pressuring settlement to save time
- **Savior complex**: Trying to "fix" the family rather than facilitating their decisions
