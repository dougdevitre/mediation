# 03 — Child Maltreatment

> Standards: 2025 Family Standard VI (Child Maltreatment); 2005 General Standard V (Confidentiality — exceptions)

## Purpose

When child maltreatment is alleged or suspected, the mediator must take appropriate steps to safeguard the child's wellbeing while navigating the intersection of confidentiality, mandatory reporting, and the mediation process.

---

## Mandatory Requirements (2025 Standard VI)

1. **Shall not** mediate cases with allegations of child maltreatment without **relevant training and expertise**
2. **Shall** explain and comply with the scope and limits of confidentiality, including **mandatory reporting requirements**
3. **Should** provide referrals to appropriate services (child protective services, counseling, legal advocacy)
4. **Should** consider suspending or terminating mediation when child maltreatment has been alleged or the mediator identifies concerns

---

## Definition

The 2025 standards use the term **"child maltreatment"** (replacing the 2000 standards' term "child abuse") to encompass:

- Physical abuse
- Sexual abuse
- Emotional/psychological abuse
- Neglect (physical, educational, medical, emotional)
- Exposure to domestic abuse

---

## Protocol When Child Maltreatment Is Alleged or Suspected

### Step 1: Assess Mediator Competence

Before proceeding, confirm:
- [ ] Do you have specific training in child maltreatment dynamics?
- [ ] Do you have expertise in recognizing signs of maltreatment?
- [ ] Are you current on your jurisdiction's mandatory reporting laws?

If **no** to any of the above → **do not mediate this aspect of the case.** Refer to a mediator with appropriate training, or suspend mediation on child-related issues.

### Step 2: Clarify Confidentiality Limits

Before or at the start of mediation, clearly explain to all parties:

- Mediator confidentiality has limits
- Mandatory reporting obligations under state/jurisdiction law
- What types of disclosures may trigger reporting
- That the mediator will comply with legal reporting requirements regardless of the mediation context

### Step 3: When Concerns Arise During Mediation

If a disclosure or observation raises child maltreatment concerns:

1. **Do not ignore it.** Acknowledge the disclosure without judgment.
2. **Assess immediacy.** Is there an immediate safety risk to a child?
3. **Consult your jurisdiction's mandatory reporting law.** Determine whether you are a mandatory reporter and whether the disclosure meets the reporting threshold.
4. **If reporting is required**: Inform the parties that you have a legal obligation to report. Do not promise confidentiality that conflicts with mandatory reporting duties.
5. **Provide referrals**: Child protective services, counseling, legal advocacy, family support services.
6. **Assess mediation appropriateness**: Consider whether to suspend or terminate mediation.

### Step 4: Decision Matrix

| Scenario | Action |
|---|---|
| Allegation made; no immediate risk; mediator is trained | Continue with heightened awareness. Provide referrals. Monitor. |
| Allegation made; mediator lacks required training | Suspend mediation on child-related issues. Refer to qualified mediator or alternative process. |
| Immediate safety risk to child | Suspend mediation. Comply with mandatory reporting. Provide safety referrals. |
| Ongoing concerns about child wellbeing | Consider suspension or termination. Provide referrals. Document concerns (within confidentiality limits). |
| Disclosure triggers mandatory reporting | Report as required by law. Inform parties of the report. Assess whether mediation can continue. |

## Mandatory Reporting — Mediator Considerations

> **Important**: Mandatory reporting laws vary by jurisdiction. This section provides general guidance. Mediators must know and follow the specific laws in their jurisdiction.

### Common Questions

**Am I a mandatory reporter?**
In many jurisdictions, mediators are mandatory reporters — especially those who are also licensed attorneys, therapists, social workers, or counselors. Some jurisdictions make all adults mandatory reporters. Check your state law.

**Does mediation confidentiality override mandatory reporting?**
Generally, **no.** Mandatory reporting statutes typically override mediation confidentiality protections. However, this varies by jurisdiction. Some mediation confidentiality statutes include explicit exceptions for mandatory reporting; others are silent.

**When should I tell the parties about reporting?**
Inform parties **before mediation begins** that confidentiality has limits, including mandatory reporting. If a report becomes necessary, inform the parties that you are making a report.

**Can I continue mediating after making a report?**
This depends on the case, the parties, and the nature of the report. Consider whether the mediation process remains safe and productive. Suspension may be appropriate.

## Connecting to Voice of the Child

When child maltreatment concerns are present, the mediator should be especially attentive to:

- Whether the child's perspective is being adequately represented
- Whether proposed parenting arrangements protect the child's safety
- Whether the child has access to supportive services
- Whether the child is being used as a messenger, spy, or weapon between the parties

→ See `references/08-voice-of-child.md` for including the child's voice appropriately.

## Training Requirements

Per Standard VI(A), mediators **shall not** mediate child maltreatment cases without:

- Training in recognizing signs of child maltreatment (physical, emotional, sexual, neglect)
- Training in mandatory reporting obligations
- Understanding of child development
- Understanding of the impact of maltreatment on children
- Familiarity with child protective services and related systems

→ See `references/15-qualifications-training.md` for full training requirements.
