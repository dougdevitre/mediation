# 18 — Understanding Your Mediation Agreement: A Plain-Language Guide

> **For parties**: This guide explains common mediation agreement terms in everyday language. It does not constitute legal advice. Always have your agreement reviewed by your own attorney before signing.

---

## Purpose

Mediation agreements often use legal terminology that can feel intimidating. This guide translates the most common provisions into plain language so you understand what you're agreeing to.

---

## Parenting Plan Terms

### Residential Schedule / Custody Schedule
**What it means**: Which parent the children live with on which days. This is the regular, repeating schedule for school-year weekdays, weekends, and overnights.

**What to ask**: "Can I see this on an actual calendar?" Use the Schedule Visualizer tool to see what different patterns look like month by month.

### Legal Custody vs. Physical Custody
- **Legal custody** = who makes the big decisions (school, medical, religion)
- **Physical custody** = where the children actually live day-to-day

These are separate. You can share legal custody (make decisions together) while one parent has primary physical custody (children live with them most of the time).

### Joint Major Decision-Making
**What it means**: Both parents must agree on big decisions — education, non-emergency healthcare, religious upbringing, and extracurricular activities. Day-to-day decisions (bedtime, meals, homework) are made by whoever has the children at the time.

**What to ask**: "What happens if we can't agree on a major decision?" Your agreement should have a process: mediation first, then a parenting coordinator or court.

### Right of First Refusal
**What it means**: If the parent who has the children needs childcare (for more than a certain number of hours), they must offer the time to the other parent first before using a babysitter or other caregiver.

**Example**: "If Mom needs to be away for more than 4 hours during her parenting time, she must first offer that time to Dad before hiring a babysitter."

### Parallel Parenting vs. Co-Parenting
- **Co-parenting** = parents communicate regularly, coordinate, attend events together, flexible with schedule
- **Parallel parenting** = parents operate independently, minimal direct communication, use a co-parenting app, follow the schedule strictly

Parallel parenting is often used when conflict is high. It's not a failure — it protects children from parental conflict.

### Supervised Visitation
**What it means**: One parent's time with the children is monitored by a third party (professional supervisor, family member, or supervised visitation center). This is usually temporary and may be stepped down over time.

### Bird-Nesting
**What it means**: The children stay in one home, and the parents rotate in and out. Less common, but can reduce disruption for children during transition periods.

## Financial Terms

### Child Support
**What it means**: A regular payment from one parent to the other to help cover the children's living expenses. Most states have guidelines that calculate the amount based on both parents' incomes and the parenting time split.

**Important**: You generally cannot waive child support entirely — it's the children's right, not the parents'. Courts must approve any deviation from guidelines.

### Spousal Support / Alimony / Maintenance
**What it means**: One spouse pays the other to help them maintain a reasonable standard of living after divorce. Several types:
- **Rehabilitative**: Temporary, while the receiving spouse gets education or job training
- **Permanent/Indefinite**: Ongoing, usually in long marriages with significant income disparity
- **Lump sum**: One-time payment instead of ongoing monthly payments

**Common termination events**: Remarriage of the receiving spouse, cohabitation with a new partner, death of either spouse, or a specific end date.

### Equitable Distribution / Property Division
**What it means**: Dividing assets (what you own) and debts (what you owe) between the two parties. "Equitable" means fair — not necessarily 50/50.

**Marital property**: Acquired during the marriage (usually divided)
**Separate property**: Owned before marriage or inherited (usually kept by original owner)

### QDRO (Qualified Domestic Relations Order)
**What it means**: A court order that splits a retirement account (401k, pension) between spouses without triggering early withdrawal penalties or taxes. You need this document prepared by an attorney if your agreement divides retirement assets.

### Offset
**What it means**: Instead of splitting a retirement account with a QDRO, one spouse keeps the full retirement account and the other spouse gets something of equal value (like more home equity or a larger share of another asset).

## Process Terms

### Mediation vs. Arbitration vs. Court
- **Mediation**: You decide. The mediator helps you talk but makes no decisions. Voluntary.
- **Arbitration**: A neutral third party decides. You present your case and they make a ruling. Binding.
- **Court/Litigation**: A judge decides after attorneys present both sides. Public record.

### Caucus / Separate Session
**What it means**: The mediator meets with each party privately. What you share in caucus is confidential — the mediator won't tell the other party unless you give permission.

### Shuttle Mediation
**What it means**: The parties are in separate rooms (or separate video sessions) and the mediator goes back and forth between them. Used when it's not safe or productive for parties to be in the same room.

### Confidentiality in Mediation
**What it means**: What's said in mediation stays in mediation. Neither party (and usually not the mediator) can use mediation discussions in court. Exceptions:
- Threats of harm to yourself or others
- Child abuse or neglect (mandatory reporting)
- The final signed agreement itself (not confidential)
- What both parties agree to share

### Self-Determination
**What it means**: It's YOUR agreement. The mediator will not tell you what to do, pressure you to settle, or decide anything for you. You can accept or reject any proposal. You can walk away at any time.

### Informed Decision-Making
**What it means**: You should have enough information to make good decisions. This includes:
- Full financial disclosure from both sides
- Time to consult with your own attorney
- Understanding of your legal rights and options
- No rushed decisions

## Agreement Terms

### Modifiable vs. Non-Modifiable
- **Modifiable**: Can be changed later if circumstances change significantly (most parenting plans and child support orders are modifiable)
- **Non-modifiable**: Cannot be changed — the terms are permanent as written

### Sunset Provision
**What it means**: A provision that automatically expires or changes at a specific date or event. Example: "Supervised visitation will step down to unsupervised after 6 months of compliance with drug testing."

### Severability
**What it means**: If a court later finds that one part of your agreement is invalid or unenforceable, the rest of the agreement still stands. The invalid part doesn't destroy the whole agreement.

### Indemnification / Hold Harmless
**What it means**: If a debt is assigned to one party in the agreement, that party promises to pay it AND protect the other party from any consequences if they don't. Example: If you're assigned the credit card debt but don't pay, your ex can hold you responsible for any damage to their credit.

### Good Faith
**What it means**: Both parties promise to act honestly, follow through on commitments, and not try to undermine the agreement. This is both a legal concept and a practical commitment.

---

## Questions to Ask Before Signing

1. **Do I understand every provision?** If not, ask the mediator to explain or consult your attorney.
2. **Can I actually comply with this?** Don't agree to something you can't do.
3. **Have I had enough time to think?** You can always ask for more time.
4. **Has my attorney reviewed this?** Strongly recommended before signing.
5. **What happens if circumstances change?** Understand the modification process.
6. **What happens if the other party doesn't comply?** Understand enforcement options.
7. **Am I signing because I want to, or because I feel pressured?** If pressured, tell the mediator — they are required to protect your self-determination.
