# 09 — Parenting Plan Development

> Standards: 2025 Family Standards I (Self-Determination), V(D) (Domestic Abuse — parenting plans), X (Voice of the Child)

## Purpose

Help mediators facilitate the development of parenting plans that protect children's physical safety and psychological wellbeing while supporting both parents' self-determination.

---

## Parenting Plan Components

### Residential Schedule
- [ ] Regular weekday and weekend schedule
- [ ] Holiday and special occasion schedule
- [ ] Summer/school break schedule
- [ ] Birthday arrangements (children's and parents')
- [ ] Religious/cultural observance schedule
- [ ] Flexibility provisions for schedule changes

### Decision-Making Authority
- [ ] Major decisions: education, healthcare, religious upbringing, extracurricular activities
- [ ] Joint vs. sole decision-making (and which areas)
- [ ] Process for resolving disagreements on major decisions
- [ ] Emergency decision-making authority
- [ ] Day-to-day decisions during each parent's time

### Communication
- [ ] Parent-to-parent communication method and frequency
- [ ] Parent-to-child communication during other parent's time
- [ ] Technology use for communication (apps, platforms)
- [ ] Dispute resolution process for parenting disagreements
- [ ] Information sharing (school records, medical records, activities)

### Transitions
- [ ] Pick-up and drop-off logistics
- [ ] Transportation responsibility
- [ ] Transition locations (especially in safety-concern cases)
- [ ] Items that travel with the child
- [ ] Protocol for late pick-ups or no-shows

### Child-Specific Provisions
- [ ] Children's activities and extracurriculars
- [ ] School and education arrangements
- [ ] Healthcare providers and insurance
- [ ] Childcare arrangements
- [ ] Special needs accommodations
- [ ] Pet arrangements
- [ ] Relationships with extended family and significant others

### Travel and Relocation
- [ ] Notice requirements for travel
- [ ] Passport possession and travel consent
- [ ] Relocation provisions and notice requirements
- [ ] Geographic restrictions (if any)

### Financial Provisions (mediation-appropriate)
- [ ] Child support (referral to attorney or guidelines calculator)
- [ ] Expense sharing for activities, medical, education
- [ ] Process for handling unexpected expenses

### Review and Modification
- [ ] Built-in review dates as children grow
- [ ] Process for requesting modifications
- [ ] Dispute resolution for future disagreements (mediation first, then court)
- [ ] Developmental milestones that trigger review

## Domestic Abuse Considerations for Parenting Plans

Per Standard V(D), when domestic abuse is present:

- **Limit opportunities for future manipulation or abuse**
- Traditional co-parenting models may not be suitable in coercive control cases
- Consider parallel parenting instead of co-parenting
- Minimize direct parent-to-parent contact
- Use supervised exchanges or public exchange locations
- Consider supervised visitation where safety requires it
- Build in protections against using the child as a communication conduit
- Include provisions that prevent one parent from monitoring or controlling the other through the child
- Specify consequences for violations

### Parallel Parenting vs. Co-Parenting

| Feature | Co-Parenting | Parallel Parenting |
|---|---|---|
| Communication | Direct, frequent, flexible | Minimal, structured, written only |
| Decision-making | Collaborative, joint | Each parent decides during their time (or specified allocation) |
| Flexibility | High | Low — detailed, specific schedules |
| Transitions | Cooperative | Structured, possibly supervised |
| Appropriate when | Both parents can communicate respectfully | Domestic abuse, high conflict, inability to communicate safely |

## Age-Appropriate Schedule Considerations

| Age | Key Factors |
|---|---|
| 0–2 years | Attachment needs; shorter, more frequent contact with non-primary parent; consistency of caregivers |
| 3–5 years | Increasing ability to handle separation; still needs routine and predictability; may struggle with transitions |
| 6–9 years | School schedule drives the plan; emerging friendships and activities; can handle longer blocks of time |
| 10–13 years | Growing independence; social life becomes important; may have strong preferences; sports/activities scheduling |
| 14–17 years | Need for autonomy; input on schedule; driving changes logistics; college/career planning; may resist rigid schedules |
