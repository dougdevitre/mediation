# 08 — Voice of the Child

> Standards: 2025 Family Standard X (Voice of the Child); 2005 General Standard VI (Quality of the Process)

## Purpose

The 2025 standards expand the focus on child-centered practice, emphasizing flexible parenting plans that evolve with children's needs and safeguards for involving children in the mediation process.

---

## Core Principle

Mediation has the potential to **amplify the voice of the child**. The mediator should help parents consider their children's perspectives, needs, and developmental stages when making decisions.

---

## Methods for Including the Child's Voice

The 2025 standards recognize multiple appropriate methods — the mediator should select based on the child's age, development, family dynamics, and safety considerations:

### Indirect Methods (through parents)
- Help parents articulate and consider each child's perspective
- Ask parents to describe each child's daily routine, preferences, relationships
- Explore how proposed arrangements would affect each child specifically
- Encourage parents to think about the transition from the child's viewpoint

### Supported Methods (through professionals)
- Child specialist or child consultant interviews the child and reports themes to mediation
- Guardian ad litem or attorney for the child provides input
- Therapist provides developmental and wellbeing information (within confidentiality limits)
- School counselor provides educational and social adjustment information

### Direct Involvement (child participates)
- Child attends a portion of mediation (with appropriate preparation and safeguards)
- Child writes a letter or creates art expressing their wishes
- Child participates via age-appropriate technology

---

## Safeguards for Direct Child Involvement

If a child is directly involved in any part of the mediation:

- [ ] Is the child's involvement voluntary? The child should never be forced to participate.
- [ ] Is the child developmentally able to participate meaningfully?
- [ ] Has the child been prepared for what to expect?
- [ ] Is there a safety concern that would make involvement inappropriate?
- [ ] Will the child be put in the position of choosing between parents?
- [ ] Is there a plan for supporting the child before, during, and after involvement?
- [ ] Has the mediator obtained specific training in child-inclusive mediation?
- [ ] Are both parents informed and in agreement about the child's involvement?

> **Critical**: The child should never be asked to make decisions, choose between parents, or bear responsibility for outcomes. The child's role is to share their experience and perspective, not to arbitrate.

---

## Developmental Considerations

| Age Range | Considerations |
|---|---|
| 0–5 years | Indirect methods only. Focus on attachment, routine, transitions. Parents describe child's needs. |
| 6–9 years | Indirect or supported methods preferred. Children may express preferences but should not be decision-makers. Concrete thinking; may not grasp legal concepts. |
| 10–13 years | Supported or carefully structured direct involvement possible. Can articulate preferences. May feel loyalty conflicts. Needs reassurance they are not choosing. |
| 14–17 years | May benefit from some direct involvement. Can understand process and express informed preferences. Still should not bear decision-making burden. Must be voluntary. |

---

## Red Flags — When NOT to Include the Child Directly

- Active domestic abuse or safety concerns
- Child is being coached or pressured by a parent
- Child is displaying significant emotional distress
- Parent is using the child as a messenger, spy, or weapon
- Child has expressed that they do not want to participate
- The mediator lacks training in child-inclusive practices
- Including the child could cause psychological harm

---

## Parenting Plan Considerations

When helping parents develop parenting plans, ensure the plan:

- Reflects each child's developmental stage and specific needs
- Provides stability and predictability for the child
- Supports the child's relationships with both parents (when safe)
- Accounts for the child's school, activities, friendships, and community
- Builds in flexibility as the child grows and needs change
- Protects the child from parental conflict
- In domestic abuse cases: limits opportunities for manipulation or continued abuse
