# 07 — Session Planning & Facilitation

> Standards: 2025 Family Standards I (Self-Determination), II (Informed Decision-Making); 2005 General Standard VI (Quality of the Process)

## Purpose

Guide the mediator through structuring and conducting mediation sessions that uphold self-determination, informed decision-making, and process quality.

---

## Session Structure Framework

### Opening
1. Welcome and introductions
2. Review ground rules and process agreements
3. Confirm confidentiality and its exceptions
4. Confirm voluntary participation (either party may withdraw)
5. Review agenda and goals for the session
6. Address any safety or logistical concerns

### Information Gathering
1. Each party shares their perspective (uninterrupted)
2. Mediator summarizes and reflects key points (neutrally)
3. Identify issues to be addressed
4. Prioritize issues collaboratively
5. Identify information gaps — facilitate disclosure or recommend expert consultation

### Option Generation
1. Brainstorm options without evaluating
2. Encourage creative solutions
3. Ensure both parties contribute
4. Consider children's needs and perspectives
5. Reference applicable legal frameworks (without giving legal advice)

### Negotiation and Decision-Making
1. Evaluate options against parties' interests and children's needs
2. Reality-test proposals
3. Address power imbalances (→ references/10)
4. Ensure decisions are informed and voluntary
5. Never pressure settlement

### Closing
1. Summarize agreements reached and issues remaining
2. Confirm next steps and homework
3. Schedule next session
4. Remind parties to consult independent counsel
5. Check in on each party's comfort and safety

---

## Facilitation Principles

**Self-determination**: The mediator assists communication and encourages understanding but does not make decisions for the parties. The mediator helps parties explore options, make decisions, and reach their own agreements.

**Informed decision-making**: Before and during mediation, allow time for parties to consult with experts and support persons. The mediator may provide information the mediator is qualified to share (e.g., process information, relevant court procedures) but shall not provide therapy or legal advice.

**Balanced participation**: Monitor and address dynamics that might undermine either party's ability to meaningfully participate.

**Child focus**: Keep children's needs and interests at the center of discussions about parenting arrangements.

---

## Separate Sessions (Caucus / Shuttle)

The mediator may hold separate sessions with individual parties. Before doing so:

- Discuss the confidentiality of separate sessions with all parties
- Obtain agreement on what (if anything) from a separate session may be shared
- Use separate sessions to assess safety, address power imbalances, or explore options privately
- In domestic abuse cases, separate sessions may be the default format

---

## When to Pause or Recess

- A party becomes visibly distressed or unable to continue
- Power imbalance becomes apparent and cannot be managed in joint session
- New information emerges that requires independent consultation
- Domestic abuse indicators emerge
- The mediator needs to assess whether mediation should continue
- The discussion becomes unproductive or circular
